## Introduction. The Conversation I Didn't Plan to Turn into a Book

It all started in the most ordinary way. I wrote a short comment for social media — about the iPhone, about automation, about how it isn't handmade production that makes a thing valuable. And I asked ChatGPT 5.4, in reasoning mode, to translate that comment into American English — for my English-speaking audience.

The model translated it. I tossed it one more remark — not about the translation this time, but about the logic. It answered. I objected. It rephrased. I pressed it from another angle.

Two hours later, we were no longer discussing the iPhone or the market. We were discussing what a human's durable advantage over artificial general intelligence might be — not over today's language models, but over an AGI that is, by definition, comparable to a human in general cognitive breadth and consistently superior to 90% of people in the quality of decisions.

Two hours after that, we were discussing insight as a catastrophic event in the mathematical sense.

Two more — the ontology of reality.

Two more after that, and I realized I was holding fifteen hundred lines of conversation in which a fairly coherent structure had assembled itself by accident — from a mundane claim about the market to an experimental program that runs straight into the foundations of physics. And the strangest part: I didn't know where I was going until I arrived.

This book is an attempt to turn that conversation into something more responsible.

I won't pretend this book was written "by me alone." Not because ChatGPT wrote it for me — it didn't, and anyone willing to take the trouble to read the original dialogue in the appendix will see for themselves where the thought actually moves and where the model is just restating what was already said, more densely. But without that partner, nothing would have happened. The machine worked as a catalyst. I'd toss out a rough formulation — it would hand back a polished one. I'd hit a weak spot — it would concede and move on to the next. And at some point I had the distinct feeling that the very format of thinking with a partner like this was already part of the argument we were building together. Because the book's central thesis ultimately turned out to be this: the machine is powerful where closed-loop work with the known is powerful. And living intelligence may be something else entirely — and in that "something else," the machine, for now, has no business.

The book is built as a hybrid. The main body is my chapters, where I lay out the theses, the arguments, and the objections. Inside each chapter are insets — short fragments from the original dialogue, at the points where my interlocutor genuinely moves the thought forward or phrases it well. The full text of the original conversation is attached at the end, in case anyone wants to see how it went without my post-editing.

I write this without being a professional scientist, philosopher, or artificial intelligence engineer. I'm an entrepreneur and a practitioner who has spent many years fascinated by the intersection of technology, evolution, and thinking. Maybe that's my weakness. Or maybe it's my strength. Big shifts in the history of ideas have rarely come from people whose job is to defend the existing paradigm. They have almost always come from the side — from people with no academic career to lose.

So — yes, this is speculation. Yes, risky in places. Yes, here and there it may not survive an encounter with serious criticism. But I believe it's better to say it out loud and take the beating than to keep it in my head and never find out what could have been done with it. If even one of this book's lines of thought turns out to be true, the consequences will be enormous. If they're all wrong, I'll still be in decent company, because most ideas in the history of thought turned out to be wrong too. Only a handful survive — and they survive precisely because someone said them out loud.

Read the book straight through and you'll see an arc. First, a narrow question: does a human have a durable market advantage over AI. Then I widen it into the question of an advantage over AGI. Then, one by one, I knock down the "refuges of humanness" — emotion, responsibility, will, risk — as untenable. Then I return to intelligence as the advantage, but in an unusual sense. Then the notion of insight as catastrophe appears. Then the metaphor of connectedness. Then a phase transition, a critical coupling. Then the ontological turn: the problem isn't AI, it's our picture of the world. And at the end — a concrete research program that proves nothing but at least lets us start looking.

The chapters are built so that each stands on its own, and you can open the book anywhere. But I recommend reading the first three in order — they clear the ground, and without that clearing, everything that follows will look like mysticism, which it is not.

One last thing. In places I write sharply, in places bluntly. I don't soften it. That's deliberate. Over the past three years, this topic has drowned in so many polite platitudes and careful hedges that sharpness here is not a stylistic gesture but an engineering necessity. You can't make out a single sharp question through a cotton blanket of caution. And sharp questions are the only reason it makes sense to take up this topic at all.

Let's start with the iPhone.

## Part I. The Question Is Posed Wrong

### Chapter 1. The iPhone and the Craftsmanship Trap

This book has a strange beginning. It grew out of an argument I didn't want.

Yet another article on the eternal theme rolled through my feed: "AI will replace everyone except people who work with their hands; only human craft will retain value; scarcity is the main source of value; if something can be automated, it will be devalued." Articles like this get published every day now. They sound soothing. They offer a simple recipe: become a plumber, a hairdresser, a sommelier, a performer — and you're safe, because you're human, and a human is by definition scarce and therefore valuable.

I wrote a short comment in response. Five paragraphs in which I tried to explain why this recipe is self-soothing nonsense that doesn't survive even a simple market test.

Here's the gist.

First — **the iPhone**. The iPhone is not made by hand. Robots assemble it, and the few human operations still left on the line can be automated at any moment, the second it gets cheaper than manual assembly. And yet the iPhone costs not less but more than the overwhelming majority of "artisanal" things in the world. The easy explanation — "it's a luxury" — doesn't work: iPhones sell in the hundreds of millions of units a year. This is not an Hermès Birkin bag made to order, one at a time. It's a mass product with a high price and colossal profit. Which means the market does not punish automation. It rewards it — when automation makes the product better, more convenient, more reliable, and higher-status.

Second — **scarcity**. The claim "scarce equals valuable" works only at a narrow edge: for a dozen luxury brands, a couple of collectible categories, and the most expensive sliver of the art world. The rest of the market field lives by an entirely different law: value is produced not by rarity but by the reliable reproduction of high quality at scale. Ask any serious investor where they'd put their money — into a company with a 100% margin and a million dollars in annual revenue, or a company with a 10% margin and ten billion in revenue. The answer needs no commentary. Which means the "craftsmanship premium" on human labor hasn't vanished entirely, but it exists only within the bounds of a narrow niche. It cannot serve as the foundation of a mass labor market.

Third — **trust**. When I go to a doctor, what I need is not "for him to be human." I need a correct diagnosis, correct treatment, and no harm done. The source of trust here is not the doctor's biological nature but a well-grounded expectation of the right result. If a robot, an AI system, or any non-biological construction shows statistically better results than a living doctor, my rational choice will shift in its favor. Not right away. First come the inertia of habit, cultural norms, fear of the new. But over time, it will shift. That is exactly how the market has always worked, and there is no reason to think AI will be any different.

Fourth — **the evolution of automation**. When something gets automated for the first time, it often works worse than the human version. The first self-driving cars drove badly. The first chatbots gave dumb answers. The first digital cameras took terrible pictures. The first washing machines shredded the laundry. But that was a temporary gap. Five, ten, fifteen years later, the automated version overtakes the human one — and overtakes it for good. This is not a hypothesis; it's a historical pattern. Betting on "humans will keep the advantage forever" means not understanding how the technology curve works.

Fifth — **the bottom line**. The consumer votes with their dollar. Faced with the choice between "a human or better quality," sooner or later they will choose quality. And if it turns out that better quality comes from AI, then they will choose AI. First in one field, then in another, then everywhere it makes sense.

That is the comment I handed the model for translation.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *1. The iPhone is not produced by a human — like a ton of other things — yet automation didn't lower its price, it raised it, because the iPhone sells not connectivity but convenience, status, and a sense of quality.*
>
> *2. Scarcity is not the key factor of value. More precisely, it works only as a very, very limited model for a narrow group of luxury brands... The consumer votes with their ruble or dollar, and if the choice is between a human and quality, sooner or later they'll prefer quality — and it's AI that will be able to deliver it.*
>
> **ChatGPT:** *(carefully translates into English)*
>
> **Me:** *What do you think? Still, the question remains open — in which industries and functions will there be demand for humans?*

And this is where the conversation I hadn't expected began.

The model answered with something standard — something about "demand for humans will persist where empathy, responsibility, trust, creativity, and leadership matter." In other words, it handed me back the very anthology of platitudes I had just pushed off from in my comment.

That answer irritated me. Not because the model is dumb — in reasoning mode it phrases things perfectly decently. But because I recognized in its answer the same form of objection I've heard a hundred times from living people. Educated, smart people, unwilling to step even an inch outside the frame of "let's emphasize the human so it's less scary."

I thought: if even an AI — one clearly trained to speak cautiously about the future of AI — produces the same cotton-wool answer, then the form of the question itself is broken. The question "in which industries will demand for humans persist" is too broad; it lets you hide in any familiar rut.

So I tried to reformulate it harder.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *This question leads to the next one — in what domain does a human have an advantage over general AI. That is exactly how the question should be posed. Not over today's language models, but over an AGI that is comparable to a human in general cognitive breadth and consistently superior to 90% of normies in the quality of decisions.*

This reformulation changes everything. Let me explain why.

When we ask "where will demand for humans persist," we're mixing two different questions. The first is sociological: where will the market keep buying human labor for any reason at all — cultural inertia, fear, habit, regulation, status. The second is more honest: where does a human have a **real advantage**, one not reducible to cultural fashion or historical lag.

The first question is boring and, unfortunately, an invitation to complacency. Yes, plumbers aren't going anywhere in the next five years. Yes, it's too early for teachers to look for a new job. Yes, therapists will be in demand for another decade or so. All of this is true — and none of it says anything about what happens beyond the horizon of cultural inertia.

The second question is the real one. It forces you to pull out of the equation everything that is not a durable advantage. Habit is not. Status is not. Fear of AI is not. Regulatory protection is not. Culture's slowness is not. All of it is temporary. All of it erodes as AI gets cheaper, more reliable, and better.

And — this is the crucial part — the second question forces us to compare ourselves not with today's chatbots but with what the artificial intelligence literature calls AGI, artificial general intelligence. A system that is, by definition, comparable to a human in general cognitive breadth and in the ability to solve problems in arbitrary domains. One that is consistently superior to 90% of people in the quality of decisions. A system that doesn't exist yet — but which, by every reasonable forecast, will appear within the next ten to twenty years.

If you honestly compare yourself with AGI rather than with GPT-4 in a browser tab, almost all the comforting answers start to crumble.

"Humans are more empathetic" — an AGI comparable to a human simulates empathy not worse but better than the average person. Because it has no bad days, no fatigue, no personal frustration to take out on a client.

"Humans are more responsible" — an AGI offers better traceability of decisions, less impulsiveness, less corruptibility, less dependence on mood. Where exactly is the human advantage here?

"Humans are more creatively flexible" — are you sure? Look at how, over the past two years, models have started solving problems that used to be considered "purely human." And these aren't even AGI yet; these are intermediate systems.

"Humans lead better" — why, exactly? Because of charisma? Charisma is a set of signaling behaviors; they can be modeled. Because a human is a "living example"? But an AGI can be present in thousands of places at once, perfectly matched to the context of each.

On closer inspection, almost every "human advantage" is either technically modelable or turns out to be not an advantage of humans as such, but our current inability to imagine an alternative.

That is the first thing you have to honestly come to terms with. And it is exactly what made me pose the question more radically: **if you remove everything that can be simulated and everything that is mere cultural inertia — what is left at all?**

The rest of this book is devoted to that question. But right here, in the first chapter, I want to pin down what seems to me the defining feature of the entire conversation that follows.

The comfortable question goes: "where will humans keep a niche." You can discuss it forever, and everyone walks away satisfied, because everyone finds the industry where they've been promised safety.

The uncomfortable question goes: "where does a human actually have a durable advantage, not reducible to anything temporary." This question may end with the answer being zero. With humans having no advantage in this sense at all. With our position being that of a temporary agent — until a better one gets built.

Or — and this is the most interesting outcome — the answer turns out to be something we weren't expecting. Not empathy, not creativity, not morality, not responsibility. Something we don't yet even know how to name properly. Something that may require revising not our relationship with AI, but our very understanding of what intelligence is and what reality is.

That is where we're headed.

But first we have to demolish what stands in the way. We have to cut off all the false refuges — to see the field clean. That's what the next two chapters do.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **ChatGPT:** *The point is strong: you're rightly shifting the question from "what AI can already do now" to "where humans will have a durable advantage even once AGI arrives." That really is the more honest framing.*

When the model said that, I knew we had finally reached a real level of conversation.

That's when everything began.
