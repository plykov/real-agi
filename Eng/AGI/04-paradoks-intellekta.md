Reaching the end of the previous chapter, I was left standing on empty ground.

All the familiar supports — empathy, love, moral intuition, responsibility, creativity, will, embodiment — turned out, one by one, to be functions. And a function, as soon as it becomes feasible to engineer, gets reproduced. I looked at that bare little patch and realized: the conversation had been going the wrong way. Not because the thinking was bad. Because the question had been framed from the start as a humanities question. "What is the value of a human being." Too much wishing for the good baked into it. I asked a different one.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *Okay, you're looking in the wrong place. Who survives?*
>
> **ChatGPT:** *Those who adapt best. But to be exact — it's not the smartest who survives, not the strongest, and not the most "authentic," but the one who reads reality faster, changes behavior faster, makes cheaper mistakes, and holds steady longer in a changing environment.*

That's a decent answer. And it only starts to work once you discard the first layer and push further. Adapts — fine, with what? In humans, that "with what" has a specific name. And I wasn't planning to announce that name — it fell out on its own in the next sentence.

What separates humans from animals is intelligence. It was intelligence that made man not just another large primate but the one who covered the planet. Not fangs. Not speed. Not fertility. Brains. Take them away — and a human becomes a physically average creature, not the hardiest and not the fastest, easy prey for a hundred species, with no chance of getting through a winter alone. Leave them in — and a hundred thousand years later he's building cities, splitting the atom, and writing books about his own limits. Everything else in a human — motivation, fear, hunger, sociality, cooperation — animals have too. Some of it better developed, some worse. But it was intelligence that made him the dominant species. Not strength, not speed, not sharp teeth. Brains.

The model tried to soften this. Intelligence, it said, is an important factor, but not the only one. There's also perception of the environment, cooperation, motivation, the transfer of experience. That's true. But it's a classic move of careful speech: introduce a list with the main thing in first place, and thereby smear the main thing across the second, third, and fourth. I didn't let that happen. A human has exactly one multiplier — intelligence. Everything else is accessories bolted on around it. And then, by my own logic, I arrived at a very unpleasant turn.

If intelligence is the main factor in human survival, then a being with more powerful intelligence gets, by definition, a claim to evolutionary advantage. AGI — a system comparable to a human in general cognitive breadth and consistently superior to 90% of people in the quality of decisions — is exactly such a being. By this logic, it should displace humans not only at work but at the very foundation. In survival.

At this point, any cautious person feels the reflex kick in to go easy on themselves. "Well, it's probably not that simple. Surely we still have something." I know that reflex and I don't trust it. Before hunting for a saving loophole, you have to walk the path of defeat honestly. The loophole, if it exists, can only be found where you have first cleanly admitted there shouldn't be one.

And I walked the path of defeat. I started conceding.

Strategy is working with a model of the world under uncertainty. AGI has a world model, has the ability to update it, has the skill of planning many moves ahead. So it won't yield on strategy. Will is holding a behavioral trajectory against interference. Strip out the romance about "strength of spirit," and it's a technical capability: not getting knocked off course under the pressure of noise and temptation. The machine is in fact better at this than we are: it isn't distracted by fatigue, isn't thrown by resentment, isn't swung toward easy gain by a flash of emotion. Will as a mechanism belongs to the machine, not to us. Risk is the collision of a model with reality under incomplete knowledge and a price for error. AGI has a model, has feedback-driven updating, has an estimate of the cost of error, has the ability to change course. So risk is available to it. More than that, the machine has no internal interference — no moods, hunger, fatigue, grudges, no fear of a particular boss — so under pressure it will most likely outperform a living human.

Add to that long-horizon strategy, multilevel planning, acting on incomplete information, recombining other people's experience into your own, transferring a skill from one task to another, holding several goals at once. All of these are modes of adaptive intelligence. If AGI is truly general — not a narrow specialist but an agent of cognitive breadth comparable to a human's — it must by definition be able to operate in these modes. Otherwise it simply isn't AGI, just another very good narrow model.

What you get is a short, brutal chain. Intelligence is the main factor in human survival. AGI surpasses humans in intelligence. All the usual evasive maneuvers — strategy, risk, will — give no fundamental advantage, because AGI commands them too. Therefore, the human has lost.

And right here I made a move that at first looks like a flat contradiction. I told the model: you're right about everything you just laid out. And yet my answer is the exact opposite.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *Intelligence is precisely the advantage over AGI. And I can prove it.*

That sounds like a blunder. Five sentences earlier I agreed that AGI surpasses humans in intelligence. Now I'm saying intelligence is the one place where humans do have an advantage. How can those two stand side by side?

They can — if the word "intelligence" doesn't mean the same thing in those two sentences.

In the first sentence — "AGI surpasses humans in intelligence" — the word is taken in its engineering sense. Speed of information processing, memory capacity, the ability to hold long context, quality of predictions, optimization of decisions within a given space of options. On that parameter, the conversation is closed. Already today, machines outperform most people in many domains. In ten or twenty years, with the arrival of AGI, they will outperform most people everywhere. To argue with that is to shut your eyes.

In the second sentence, the word is taken differently. Not as the capacity to compute inside an already given field. As the capacity to create that field. Not the horsepower for working with the known, but the ability to see what doesn't yet exist. Not a solver — but the one who poses what never existed as a problem before.

The model tried to package this shift into a smooth formula. "The human advantage lies not in the power of solving but in the power of problematization." Meaning, supposedly, that a human is strong not because he answers given questions better, but because he poses new ones himself. A pretty formula. It turns the head in the right direction — from the answer to the posing. I accepted it as a working approximation and immediately set it aside. Because "problematization" is an academic word; it hides the concrete mechanism behind a smooth wrapper. Poses new questions — how, exactly? Sees what isn't there — at what moment, and by what means? If you don't answer that, the formula remains a declaration. And declarations don't fly in this book.

So I went in harder.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *You're close. AI doesn't have insights. Humans do. Insight has to be looked at through the lens of catastrophe theory — it is a catastrophic event in the mathematical sense, when an idea arises even though there wasn't sufficient context. For AI, the volume of training is always finite, however enormous, and that is its limitation. During an insight, a human arrives at the solution by an entirely different route: the solution and the idea come all at once, and afterward the person decodes it and even finds connections to known contexts.*

Here I need to introduce two terms without which we can't move forward. Both become clear after a short explanation.

The first is insight. A familiar word, but I'm not using it in the everyday sense of a "flash of inspiration." I'm using it strictly. An insight is the moment when a person receives the solution to a problem before they can explain it. The solution arrives whole, as a finished structure. The explanation of how it came together is constructed later, after the fact, when the thought has already happened. It's not "thought fast." It's "didn't think — and there it was."

The second is catastrophe theory. A branch of mathematics developed by René Thom in the second half of the twentieth century. The word "catastrophe" here means nothing bad. It's a neutral term. It describes a discrete jump in a system's behavior under continuous change of its parameters. In everyday terms, it's the situation where you compress a spring slowly, slowly, slowly — and then it suddenly snaps. It doesn't deform smoothly. It snaps. The state "intact spring" passes into the state "broken spring" in a single leap. There is nothing in between.

So then: insight is a catastrophe in this mathematical sense. Not a smooth approach to a solution but a jump. There was one mode of thought — and then there was another. And — this is the main point — the jump happens under context insufficiency. That is, the system does not contain enough data for the solution to be derived from it in the ordinary way. And yet the solution appears.

To make clearer how insight differs from simply "thought fast," recall an everyday situation. You grind away at a problem for a long time. You try one approach, a second, a third. Dead end. You set it aside. You go wash the dishes, ride the subway, brush your teeth — you do anything but the problem itself. And suddenly, without warning, in a single second, the answer assembles itself whole in your head. Not "the next reasonable step." A finished figure. You haven't even had time to check it — and you already know it's right. And only later, in hindsight, do you sit down and write out the proof step by step so it can be shown to someone else. That hindsight is not the thinking itself. It's the translation of the thinking back into the linear form in which a thought can be transmitted. The thinking itself, in that second, happened without linearity.

At this point the model tried to reassure me. It offered a safe frame: maybe insight is just hidden computation. The brain had been crunching something in the shadows for a long time and handed consciousness only the finished result. A very convenient frame. Inside it, nothing surprising happens: there was enough computation, it just ran out of the light. The picture of the world in which all thinking reduces, in principle, to operating on values by fixed rules stays untouched. You get your miracle without leaving the house.

I rejected that frame.

Not because I'm an opponent of the computational picture of the world. But because it doesn't cover what I'm talking about. The main point is not the subjective suddenness of insight. Not that it *seems* to a person as if the solution came all at once. The main point is context insufficiency. The fact that the system did not have enough material for the solution to be derived from it — and the solution appeared anyway.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *The weak part is right here — "the idea arises out of nowhere" and "there wasn't sufficient context." You ignored that thesis, and it's the main one, because the meaning of insight leads us to an entirely different physical picture of the world.*

If that thesis is true, then insight is not "accelerated hidden processing." Then it's something else. Something not described by the formula "state A plus a transition rule equals state B." Something that doesn't fit the standard picture in which a system's next state is fully generated from its previous state by fixed rules.

Let me say it plainly: I am not proving this. I am asserting it. The proof, if it's possible at all, is years of work ahead, for people with different training and different tools. I'm doing what anyone who talks seriously about the difference between humans and AGI is obligated to do: naming the place where, by my sense of it, the real boundary runs, and formulating it so the boundary can be searched for, tested, refuted. Not "we possess something ineffable." Rather: here is the point, here is the phenomenon, here is its property, here is the consequence.

And now let's return to the paradox this chapter opened with.

"Intelligence is precisely the human advantage over AGI" — this is not about humans computing better or searching faster. On that front, AGI wins outright. It's about the fact that human thinking sometimes operates in a mode that doesn't reduce to search at all. Sometimes it doesn't take the next step inside the space of possible solutions — it cracks open the space itself. A new structure arises that was not in the source data.

AGI, by its very construction — even hypothetical, even arbitrarily powerful — is a machine trained on a finite volume of data. Colossal, but finite. It works inside that volume. It can generalize, recombine, extrapolate, optimize. It can expand the volume by further training on new data. But everything it does is movement inside the space defined by its training. It does not step past the boundary of what already exists, at least in embryo, in its corpus.

A human, sometimes, does. Not often. Not always. Most people go their whole lives without a single true insight in the strict sense, and that's normal. Most of human life is work inside an already familiar frame — exactly the work AGI will do better than we do. But the points at which human thought generates something that was not in its source data — those points exist in history. They are rare. They produce a disproportionately large effect. Because they are precisely what changes the entire space of problems in which thousands of people and thousands of machines then do their ordinary work.

The paradox rests on the fact that in everyday speech the word "intelligence" conflates two fundamentally different things: the capacity to work well with the known and the capacity to produce the unknown. AGI takes the first away from humans. Humans keep the second. And the second is not a leftover, not a consolation prize. Not a ledge to stand on so you don't fall all the way down. The second is, possibly, the only place where intelligence in the true sense of the word exists at all.

That is my interim formula. And that is the boundary where I will stop in this chapter.

The paradox is posed. I have a word I never allowed myself to use in the strict sense before — insight. And that word has a property that makes even me uncomfortable: context insufficiency. A solution for which there was no material. This is not slick rhetoric, and it's not a way to keep a cozy corner reserved for humans. It's a pointer to the specific place where our usual picture of "how thinking works" stops adding up. Stops adding up not in my head, but in the phenomenon itself, if you look at it honestly.

What to do with that place next is a separate conversation. I am not starting it in this chapter.
