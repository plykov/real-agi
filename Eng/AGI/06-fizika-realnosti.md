The argument over whether a machine can reproduce human thinking almost always collapses, fast, into an argument about computation. Digital or analog. Discrete or continuous. Finite-state machine or dynamical system. It seems like picking the right side would settle the question. In fact, every one of these moves stays inside the same frame. The real question is not which physics of computation is more correct. The real question is about the frame itself.

The word "paradigm" is worth using here in its plain sense, not the scholarly one. A paradigm is a frame of description through which an entire era tries to explain everything in sight. The ancient world explained everything through the elements: fire, water, earth, air. The mechanics of the early modern age explained everything through the machine: the universe a clock, man a hydraulic automaton, society a spring. The nineteenth century found electromagnetism — and within two decades people were seriously discussing "vital fields," "nerve currents," and the "electricity of thought." The twentieth century launched cybernetics — and the whole world suddenly turned out to be built of signal, feedback, and control. Today that role belongs to computation. Everything has become an algorithm, information, a state.

Every time, the picture repeats itself. A powerful insight appears — a specific model that, for the first time, explains something well. Then that model starts getting projected onto everything else — whether or not it has anything to do with that everything else. And every time, it later turns out the projection was partial. There is no special reality behind "mechanism," "field," "signal," "computation." These are ways of describing. Metaphors, in the strict sense of the word — narratives that make things convenient to talk about. The convenience is real. But any metaphor is a frame, and reality is wider than the frame.

Serious people work inside today's computational frame. Deutsch builds his theory of the universal computer on it. Tegmark pushes it all the way to the claim that the universe is a mathematical structure. Wolfram derives from it the program of a new kind of science built on cellular automata. These are strong constructions, and I'm not going to argue with them here. I want to point at something else: the mere fact that you can build an impressive construction inside a frame does not make the frame mandatory. Inside the ancient picture of the elements, people built impressive constructions too — for two thousand years.

Now, about the word "computation" and the trap it carries today. When you say "computation," what people hear by default is a finite-state machine: a device that changes discrete internal states step by step according to fixed rules. That is the model that gets silently substituted in. And yet that model is a special case.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *What gets in people's way here is the term "computation" as a process of changing discrete states — a finite-state machine. But before that, there were analog computers.*

Before digital machines there were analog computers — devices in which quantities are represented not by digits but by continuous physical signals: voltage, current, the position of a shaft. They did not calculate in the discrete sense — they became the physical embodiment of an equation and solved it by what happened inside them. There was Vannevar Bush's American Differential Analyzer; there was the Soviet MN-7. They existed and they worked. Today they are almost forgotten, and that's a shame — they remind us that computation as a class is wider than the single example the word has fused with.

The temptation at this point is to widen the frame. To say: maybe intelligence isn't digital, but analog, dynamical, topological. The frame has widened. But that is not a way out of the argument. It's cosmetics. I am still claiming that thinking is a specially organized computational process, just a more exotic one. I remain inside the same paradigm — I've merely moved from one of its compartments to another. An analog computer and a digital computer are two different metaphors inside one frame. Different machines, different physical embodiments, different conclusions — but the same type of description.

There is an even deeper example of the same mistake, and it almost never gets discussed because it's too familiar. Topological metaphors. We have lived in three-dimensional space since childhood, and it sits so deep in our subconscious that we stop noticing it. So when we try to depict anything at all — a neural network, an idea, relationships, causality, the states of a system — we automatically draw volumetric diagrams. Bubbles of connections. Graphs. Multidimensional parameter spaces. Energy landscapes with hills and valleys. Decision surfaces. It feels like we're describing how the phenomenon is built. In fact, we are projecting our three-dimensional body onto an abstract object that may have nothing in common with three-dimensionality at all. This is a metaphor again, and an especially dangerous one, because it formalizes. Formalization masks the fact that what we described was not the object but our own projection onto it.

This is the methodological move that makes this whole conversation worth having. Before tackling complex phenomena — consciousness, insight, intelligence — you need to notice, at least once, that all our descriptions are built from inside some frame, and try to step out of it. Not swap one frame for another from the same list. Notice the list itself and take a step back. If you don't, the frame seeps into the very logic of everything that follows, and everything downstream gets colored by it automatically. No amount of internal rigor will fix that afterward. The systemic error gets laid into the foundation.

I am not letting mysticism in through this door. Say "reality is wider than we think," and two sentences later you've got the astral plane, subtle matter, and spiritual realms. That's a different door, and it isn't mine. I'm talking about something boring, engineering-grade, historically verified: every era considered its picture final, and every era was wrong.

One supporting signal that something in our current frame doesn't add up. The human brain runs on roughly twenty watts of power. That's less than the light bulb in your refrigerator. On those twenty watts it does what modern machine-learning models reproduce only on data-center racks drawing many orders of magnitude more. And it isn't even a specifically human phenomenon — a mouse brain, at its own scale, is just as obscenely efficient. The gap is too large to write off as a lucky engineering optimization. When two implementations of the same function differ not by multiples or percentages but by orders of magnitude, it usually means they are doing different things under the same name. Not "one is better tuned than the other." But "one of them, possibly, isn't computation in our sense at all."

This is not a trophy for humans. It is an anomaly — the 20-watt anomaly. And the most interesting explanation of it goes like this: perhaps our computational picture of the world fails to account for something essential about how a living system is built. Not "the brain has an interesting substrate." But that the reality the brain operates in is wider than anything the computational frame is even capable of admitting.

This is where the main turn happens, and it needs to be separated from a neighboring thesis, because the two often stick together into one.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *You're falling into the terminology trap again. Not "a different physics of computation" — a different physics of reality.*

This is not wordplay. This is the difference between two completely different levels of argument.

"A different physics of computation" is an argument inside a picture of the world in which reality is still described as a set of states with local transition rules. The rules may simply be continuous, nonlinear, phase-based. A technical question. Hard, but solvable in principle. An engineers' argument.

"A different physics of reality" is an argument about the picture itself. About whether the world is exhausted by computational description in principle. Not "which kind of computation to describe it with," but "whether computation is enough at all." That is no longer an engineers' argument. It is an ontological argument — about what reality is made of. Ontology is a notion of what reality is made of in the first place; if you don't like the word, say "picture of the world" — same meaning.

A brief word about proof belongs here. AGI — a system comparable to a human in general cognitive breadth and consistently superior to 90% of people in the quality of decisions — does not yet exist. There is nothing to compare against. So formally, nothing about its limits can be proven right now, and I'm not pretending I can. But that doesn't cancel the argument. It just changes its subject. We are arguing not about what exactly a nonexistent machine will do, but about two different pictures of the world inside which it is being built. In one, intelligence is computation, and then the question is only engineering details. In the other, intelligence includes something that computation cannot exhaust, and then no amount of machine power will reproduce it.

Inside the first framing, humans are doomed: sooner or later AGI will overtake them on every parameter the computational frame is even capable of registering. Inside the second, the question stays open — and not because humans are especially good at anything, but because the very object of comparison may have been chosen wrong. The ruler is the wrong one. We're measuring the wrong thing with it.

What comes next is not persuasion. What comes next is understanding how to even handle a hypothesis of this order so that it amounts to work.
