Let me start with a little story that's almost half a century old.

In the seventies, when the first ATMs began showing up in banks, serious people were saying serious things. That a machine would never replace a teller. That the trust between a client and a bank rested on a living person behind the counter. That the elderly would never entrust their savings to a metal box. That in moments of doubt you need a living voice, a living gaze, a living word. That an ATM was for technical operations, while a relationship with a bank was about a human being. I've read texts like that. They sounded reasonable. They were signed by respected economists, psychologists, marketers.

Today, in any major city in the world, there's an ATM on every corner, and a line at a bank branch is an event. Not because we have less money or less trust. But because the question "should I trust the metal box" turned out not to be a question of trust at all. It was a question of habit. The habit went away. The trust moved.

I'm retelling this story not because it's pretty. I'm retelling it because almost every argument used in the seventies to defend the teller is back in circulation today. Only now it's been aimed at defending the human as such. Empathy. Trust. Responsibility. Creativity. Will. Embodiment. Ethical sense. The right to decide. Seven names under which one and the same claim usually hides: "the human is needed because he is human." And seven names that I intend, in this chapter, to hold up to the light one by one.

I'm not doing this with any glee. I take no pleasure in the fact that they don't hold up. Most of them are things I value myself. But our question isn't "do I like this support" — it's "will it bear the weight." A support that looks sturdy as long as no one has sat on it isn't a support; it's a stage prop. And before going looking for the real one, you have to honestly test the props.

Let me restate the frame. AGI is a system comparable to a human in general cognitive breadth and consistently superior to 90% of people in the quality of decisions. Meaning, this is not a smarter chatbot. It is a full-fledged rival at any task that can be posed at all. In Chapter One I gave up the consolation of "I'm better than GPT." Here I give up the next, deeper consolation: "I'm better than any machine at what's human." I'll take the seven familiar supports one at a time, without arranging them into a tidy list, because each one comes apart in its own way.

I'll start with the human being loved as a source of feelings. Three things usually get stacked here at once: empathy, love, and moral sense. Different in form. In essence — one and the same.

Empathy is when another person picks up on what's happening with you and responds. Not "understands in general terms." Catches the specific turn in your mood, adjusts their tone, pauses where a pause is needed, softens where softening is needed. The accepted view is that this is narrowly human. The machine, they say, won't "truly feel" it. Whether it feels it or not, I don't know — and that's not the market's question. The market's question is this: does the machine produce in you the feeling of being understood? And here everything gets much simpler. A good live conversation partner produces that feeling in maybe one out of ten of the people they land on. With the rest, it's a miss: tired, not tuned in, in a mood, not interested. A machine with no fatigue, no bad days, no personal frustration, landing in your context with a precision only a handful of people can deliver in live conversation — produces that feeling more often. Not "more deeply" — more often and more consistently. And that is quite enough. Because the person who seeks out a sensitive listener isn't going for depth — they're going to feel better. And they'll feel better with whoever knows how to make that happen, not with whoever happens to be alive.

Back in the middle of the last century, Turing proposed a simple test: if, in written correspondence, you can't tell the machine from a human, then for the practical purposes of conversation it behaves like a human. The objection to this is well known and just as old. Searle, a philosopher, came up with the image of the Chinese Room: a man who knows no Chinese follows instructions to produce the correct characters and, from the outside, is indistinguishable from someone who understands. Searle's objection was that outward correspondence doesn't prove understanding: correct on the outside, empty on the inside. Perhaps, philosophically, that's an interesting argument. But for the market and for society, "correct on the outside" is all that's needed. No one hires a doctor because he inwardly agonizes over the diagnosis. They hire him because the diagnosis is right. No one loves a partner because their neurons are of a certain type. They love them because being with them feels good. The "empty inside" genre makes sense only as long as the outside comes out worse. Once the outside comes out better, it stops working as a defense.

Love is the same thing, only stronger. I'm not going to play the romantic here. Love, if you take it apart as a mechanism, is the production of certain states in two people: warmth, recognition, desire, being needed, safety, interest. You don't fall in love with a questionnaire or with biology. You fall in love with the fact that next to this person you become a particular version of yourself. If next to some other source you become the same self, or a better one — biology loses its monopoly. At this point the objection always comes: "but come on, it won't be real." And what is "real"? That which is felt? Then the machine that produces the needed feelings more reliably wins. That which is biologically actual? Then we're talking not about love but about reproduction, and that's a different conversation. Right here I honestly told the other author of the dialogue, in writing, that separating love from sensations is a con.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *I've considered all three aspects, and none of them work, in my view, because you're not looking at them dynamically. Everything listed is nothing more than generational narratives that play out as patterns of preference. Everything about love is a need not for a person but for sensations, and if AI can provide them — and it can — there will be almost no human left here.*
>
> **ChatGPT:** *Love, friendship, empathy, attachment — under functional disassembly, these are not a cult of the biological carrier but the production of certain states: warmth, recognition, arousal, safety, interest, being needed, co-presence. And if AI can produce these states more reliably, more precisely, more cheaply, and in more personalized form, then the thesis "people will be loved because they are human" turns into nothing more than a sentimental residue of a transitional era.*

At first it will be an authenticity fetish — "well, my wife is a living one." Then the fetish will shrink into a niche, the way handcrafted bags shrank into a niche after mass production arrived. Then the niche will remain as a cultural preserve for those who need to be unlike everyone else. And that's it. I'm not saying this will happen tomorrow. I'm saying this process has no point at which you can say "from here on, biology holds by right." Biology holds by habit — like the teller in the seventies.

The third thing in this series is moral intuition. Also an everyday notion; no philosophical terms needed here. Moral intuition is when, in an unfamiliar situation, you quickly grasp what's okay and what isn't, without reasoning your way to it. A child falls into a fountain — without thinking, you jump in. A coworker cracks a vile joke — without thinking, you shut him down. Where does this speed come from? From tens of thousands of lived situations, from how you were raised, from what got approved and what got punished a thousand times before. It's a trained mechanism of rapid appraisal. A machine trained on an enormous corpus of human situations, on the breakdown of what counted as acceptable in each one and what didn't, acquires the same trained mechanism. The difference comes down to one thing: the machine has no day when it was hungry and angry and let something slide that, in its normal state, it wouldn't have. A living person has days like that. I'm not claiming the machine will be "more moral." I'm claiming its moral response is, on average, more stable. And "on average" is exactly the criterion by which society chooses over the long run.

The objection "but a human feels morally, while a machine imitates" fails here with the same impotence as Searle's did one floor up. For society, the difference between "feels" and "imitates" disappears the moment the statistics on decisions tip in the machine's favor.

Next comes a different support, heavier-looking. The human is needed not as one who feels, but as one who can be called to account. This is about responsibility.

Responsibility is when there's someone to call to account. If something goes wrong, you need a guilty party. The guilty party will explain himself, take the punishment — in the worst case go to prison, in the best case pay. Without this, they say, society falls apart. A machine cannot be guilty. Therefore the machine can't be allowed anywhere there's a price for error. Therefore the human is irreplaceable wherever there are consequences.

Sounds convincing. But look closer, and two different questions have been swapped here. People don't need a guilty party. People need decisions to be made reliably and honestly, and they need a mechanism of correction when something goes wrong. The guilty party is the historically evolved way of getting both. Precisely because, for a long time, no other mechanisms existed. There's a person — he signed off — he answers for it — therefore he'll try his best. It's blackmail by fear, and it worked as long as nothing better had come along.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *The responsibility part is funny. People don't need responsibility; people need reliability and honesty, and responsibility is a way of getting those through fear. AI can deliver decisions that are more reliable and more honest — and more effective at that.*
>
> **ChatGPT:** *Responsibility in itself is of no use to anyone. It is not a value but a proxy mechanism. People don't need a "guilty party"; people need a predictable and honest decision-making loop. It's just that, historically, humanity achieved this through personal responsibility, because no other reliable mechanisms existed.*

Better mechanisms exist. Today an airplane flies most of the flight on autopilot. If it crashes, it's not "the captain's fault." The investigation works out which component failed, which procedure was violated on the ground, which instruction turned out to be incomplete, which part of the software glitched. The culprit becomes not a person but the place in the system where the system proved weak. This is not an absence of responsibility — this is responsibility redistributed among designers, certifiers, operators, and procedures. And that's exactly why airplanes today are the safest transport in history. Not because someone with stripes on his shoulders is sitting in the cockpit. But because the system of verification is built so that failure is rare, and when it happens, it gets analyzed and closed out.

When an AI system makes a medical decision and it turns out to be wrong, we have a choice. We can say: "the machine can't be let near the patient, because there's no one to lock up." Or we can build around it the same system that's built around the airplane: pre-registration of the model, validation on a test corpus, a reporting protocol, an audit of decisions, updates driven by errors, division of responsibility among developer, verifier, and operator. This isn't exotic. It already works in aviation, in pharmaceuticals, in nuclear power. Not perfectly everywhere, but noticeably better than "the doctor signed off, and if anything happens, the doctor goes to prison." In most cases the doctor doesn't go to prison, the error remains an error, the patient remains dead. The whole of "responsibility" comes down to there being someone to feel sorry for on the news.

The objection "but a machine cannot bear moral responsibility" is still confusing the concept with the function. Moral responsibility is an emotional add-on, needed to squeeze quality out of a human. The machine doesn't need that add-on. Quality gets squeezed out of it by the design itself and its checks. We don't demand that a crane "bear moral responsibility for the load it lifts." We demand that the structure hold, that procedures be followed, that the operator be certified. And the crane isn't insulted.

A related storyline is legitimacy. This is no longer about who answers, but about whom society permits to decide. When the machine treats better than a doctor, and when it judges more reliably — and neither scenario is science fiction anymore — the institutional argument stands its ground: there is still the right to make decisions about another human being. And that right, they say, society will hand to no one but its own kind. Because the one who decides must be in the same skin himself. Must be mortal, vulnerable, must have a stake.

This is a strong argument. And the only one of the seven that, in my view, will actually hold for a while. But it will hold not as a human advantage — it will hold as an institutional brake. That is, this isn't about the human being able to do something the machine can't. It's about society not yet being ready to hand the machine the levers — and it won't hand them over in a single day. It will hand them over gradually. First at low stakes: insurance allocation, credit scoring, ambulance routing. Then at medium stakes: primary medical diagnosis, legal opinions, supplier ratings. Then at high stakes — and there will be a long fight there, and a lot of noise there, and there, most likely, regulatory restrictions will appear specifically designed to keep the human in key positions. All of that will happen. But it's a political story, not an intellectual one. It's about how slowly power gets rebuilt, not about power possessing a mind the machine lacks. In twenty years the same debate will be conducted from a different starting line. In fifty — from a third. There's no stable foundation here, only the inertia of institutions. And inertia, over the long run, is the same habit that was used to defend tellers in the seventies. Just on a bigger scale.

That leaves the last thing — where the most confident objection usually hides: that the human is stronger as an acting and deciding agent in the living world. Stacked here are creativity, will with risk, and embodiment. One at a time.

Creativity. At the everyday level, it's the ability to come up with something that didn't exist before. A new song. A new design. A new way to prepare an ordinary product. The accepted view is that this is narrowly human. Accepted — yes. But over the last two years, anyone paying close attention has seen it: systems have learned to do things that just five years ago were classed as purely human. They assemble melodies in a style. They write coherent screenplays. They solve problems that in school counted as tests of ingenuity. And this is not yet AGI. This is transitional technology. What they can do is recombine the space of already existing variants. At what level they can do it — that grows every month. It's clear that within this space, the machine will sooner or later become stronger than almost any living author. Not "more brilliant" — just more consistent: 1,000 decent songs an hour, 100 passable screenplays a day, 50 workable designs for a single brief. Most of the mass market is exactly that — "decent, consistent, cheap." Most of the mass market, the machine will take.

I know the objection that's on the tip of your tongue right now: "but that's not real creativity; the real thing is something else, something higher, something that doesn't come from permutation." Perhaps. I don't deny it. In this chapter I'm taking apart the ordinary supports, and the ordinary support "humans are creatively richer" is untenable for the market. What there is beyond the ordinary support is a separate story, and I'll get to it. But not here.

Will and risk. This one I approached in the dialogue myself, with the assumption that here, at least, something would survive.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *Well, clearly intelligence is a complex factor, because it provides not just tactical ad hoc behavior, not just solving current problems, but the ability to choose strategic paths, including under uncertainty — through will, risk, and the like. But AGI can do all of that too. For risk is simply a stream of reality tests. Right?*
>
> **ChatGPT:** *If we cut completely cold, then risk, will, resolve, action under uncertainty are not magic but work with incomplete information under the pressure of consequences. And in that sense there's no obvious reason why AGI couldn't do this. If we define risk strictly, then risk is simply a model colliding with reality under incomplete knowledge and a price of error. Will, in its dry form, is not "strength of soul" but the capacity to hold a behavioral trajectory in spite of local interference.*

I spent a long time trying to figure out how to argue with this, and came up with nothing. Strip risk of its poetic wrapping, and it's work with incomplete information under the pressure of consequences. AGI has a model of the world. It has updating from feedback. It has the capacity to weigh options under uncertainty. It has an estimate of the price of error. It has the ability to change course. Out of what, exactly, is the living human's advantage supposed to grow here? Out of the fact that a human feels fear? That's more of a minus. Fear is a biologically ancient system, tuned for tigers in the bushes, and under modern conditions it hinders more often than it helps. A market speculator whose fear is firing at full strength loses to a calm algorithm. A surgeon whose hands shake at the risk loses to a robotic system with tremor suppression. A pilot under stress is worse than the autopilot — and the autopilot is the earliest and most primitive form of machine agent. What AGI's will turns out to be is a separate question, but the rhetoric of "the machine will have no spirit" wins nothing back here. If will is holding course despite whatever knocks you off course, then it is, first, formalizable, and second, the machine should be better at it, because it has no internal interference — no moods, no grudges, no hunger, no fatigue.

That leaves the body. In the West, that's exactly the word most often used: *embodiment*. The idea is that a human thinks not only with the head but with the whole body, that intelligence resides in the muscles, in gestures, in how a person stands and how they turn, in the experience of pressure, hunger, pain, fatigue. This is not an empty argument. Much of human thinking is indeed tied to the fact that we have hands, legs, skin, and nerves. But as a defense of the human against AGI, it works only as long as AGI stays disembodied. And nothing forbids it from acquiring a body, sensors, actuators, feedback from the environment. All of this is already being built. Robotics lags language models by a decade or so, but it's tracking the same curve. If AGI gets a body, it will have its own embodiment. Not like ours — different. But "a different embodiment" does not mean "no embodiment." The advantage here is, once again, temporary, not structural.

And right here let me add something — for those who'll say "but AGI doesn't suffer, doesn't tire, isn't vulnerable." That is at once its weakness and its strength. The weakness — yes, the emotional context built up through suffering will be different for it. The strength — it doesn't break down because it's having a bad morning, can't be gotten to through loved ones, doesn't lose its skills to burnout. In any profession where vulnerability today counts as "a plus, because it makes you kin to the client," it's a plus exactly until the alternative shows that the same work can be done better without the vulnerability.

Creativity, will, risk, embodiment — these are all concrete ways a living mind copes with a world that keeps throwing up new things. If AGI is genuinely general — that is, capable across the board rather than in one narrow lane — it must be capable of these too. Not better than a human in every case, but on average — yes, better.

Time to draw the line. Seven names, seven teardowns, and nowhere did my foot find bottom. Each time, the same thing came to light: what we called "human" in the human turns out, under functional disassembly, to be a function. And a function, by definition, can be produced some other way — as soon as it becomes possible, in engineering terms, to produce it some other way. I won't go back through which support showed what — anyone who wants a refresher can flip back. The state matters more than the list. The state is: empty.

I understand how this may sound. As if I'm stripping the human for parts and saying "everything's replaceable." But that's not what I'm saying. I'm saying a simple thing: the parts we've grown used to treating as supports and presenting as advantages aren't supports at all. They rest on a historical head start. Over the long run, that head start runs out.

So what does that leave us with, then.

If you remove everything that can be simulated and everything that is cultural inertia — what remains in the human at all, anything one can stand on in the face of AGI? Emotions — gone. Ethics — gone. Creativity — gone. Will — gone. Body — gone. Institutions — gone. Habit — gone. Each of these supports, tested honestly, reveals a function inside itself, and a function is reproducible.

What's left is an empty place.

And that empty place is where I found myself, having reached this point in the conversation. Not in despair and not in rapture. In a fairly cold, working state. One thing remained — the thing all seven familiar hands had been reaching past. It has to be approached separately.
