The last chapter ended in a very uncomfortable place. I agreed that insight is a catastrophe under insufficient context — like a compressed spring that snaps without intermediate stages — and in doing so I handed myself a problem that wouldn't leave me alone for a long time. If a system doesn't contain enough material to derive a solution, and the solution shows up anyway — where does it come from. Not as an experience, but literally. Through what channel.

This question sounds like a bid for mysticism, which is exactly why most respectable people don't ask it. Better to keep quiet than to look like someone who believes in something weird. I'm not going to keep quiet, but I don't want mysticism either. So I need a metaphor that will hold the question within the bounds of engineering language — and at the same time not smear it into banality.

I offered the model such a metaphor. It turned out better than I myself expected.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *Let me give you a metaphor. Look, imagine two computers. One is a super powerful computer — very powerful, top of the line. It has a gigantic database, a gigantic context. It runs the most powerful artificial intelligence and so on. But it's not connected to the internet. The other is an ordinary iPhone, with a pile of limitations, but connected to the internet. Which one has more potential?*
>
> **ChatGPT:** *The iPhone. Not because it's stronger, but because it's connected to a source of context it doesn't contain.*

This looks like a simple engineering puzzle. In fact, it's a switch of criteria.

When we argue about which is more powerful — a human or AGI, an artificial general intelligence comparable to a human in general cognitive breadth and consistently superior to 90% of people in the quality of decisions — we measure compute by default. How many parameters. How much memory. What speed. What accuracy. On that scale the offline supercomputer beats the iPhone hands down. It has orders of magnitude more of everything we're used to calling "power."

And still — the iPhone has more potential. Because the supercomputer is closed in on its own contents, while the iPhone is connected to something that isn't inside it. Power isn't determined by local horsepower. Power is determined by whether the system is closed or not.

That's the switch. If you take it seriously, the entire previous argument about humans and AGI was an argument on the wrong scale. We were comparing two devices by compute, when the decisive property is openness versus closedness. And then it turns out that a human, who simply has no chance against AGI on compute, can win in a different dimension altogether: not in how much he computes, but in the fact that he may not be computationally closed.

Next I have to say what "not closed" means. This is where the delicate part begins.

In the last chapter I rejected the frame "insight is just hidden computation." Now let me refine that. I rejected it not because I believe no computation happens at the moment of insight. Something surely happens. The brain works, neurons fire, processes run that could in principle be described somehow in computational terms. That's not what the dispute is about. The dispute is about something else.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *Insight may be a hidden computation, but the key thing here isn't the fact itself — it's whether it's connected to the internet or not. If we take as a baseline hypothesis the idea that a human is connected to something larger, something that at first glance seems immanent, then maybe some hidden computations do exist — but they produce a qualitative leap. And that hidden computation isn't even the tip of the iceberg; it's the dust on it.*
>
> **ChatGPT:** *The question isn't whether there's a computation underneath insight. The question is whether human intelligence is a computationally closed system. If it isn't, the whole argument about AI horsepower goes sideways. Because even an infinitely powerful closed system can lose to a weak but connected one.*

I accepted this reformulation immediately, because it puts in place everything I'd previously been trying to explain in circles.

Hidden computation as an explanation of insight is an attempt to preserve a cozy picture of the world where thinking fits entirely inside the skull. Computed in the shadows, delivered to consciousness. Miracle explained, everyone can go home. The trouble is that this frame silently assumes the human is a closed system. That all the material a thought is assembled from already sits inside him, just out of sight. If that's true, then hidden computation really does explain insight exhaustively.

And if it's not true — it explains nothing. It describes the dust on the iceberg without knowing the iceberg is there.

Here I have to be careful not to careen off into outright madness. I'm not claiming the brain does no computing. I'm not claiming insight is the retrieval of a ready-made answer from some external database. I'm not claiming AGI can never, in principle, plug into this. And, above all, I'm not claiming that the very existence of a "wider layer of reality" has already been proven. These are all hypotheses, and every one of them is currently open.

I'm claiming one thing: the argument "is there a hidden computation or not" is an argument about the wrong thing. The real argument is about whether human intelligence is closed or not. And in that form it becomes meaningful.

If a human is not closed — then his power lies not in compute but in connectedness. Then insight is not the peak moment of a local processor's work, but the moment when something enters the local system that was not derivable inside it. Not an answer from outside, not a message, not data. But something it gained access to.

And here I make a move that I myself consider speculative, but I see no way to dodge it.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *Yes, "Then a human in your model is not a 'biological computer with glitches' but a local interface to a wider level of reality." And here we arrive at the completely crazy hypothesis that, perhaps, at a wider level of reality computation simply isn't required, for the reason that all states, all narratives are already present. Everything is possible; there are no physical constraints. So connecting to such a level of reality requires no computation in the usual sense — you simply receive it all at once.*

This is, of course, a hypothesis of a very strong sort. I'm not going to prove it now. For now I'm simply laying it on the table and noting that it has one useful property: it explains why insight is experienced the way it's experienced. The solution arrives ready-made, whole, before any explanation. Not because the brain suddenly sped up a hundred thousand times and ran an enormous search in a millisecond — but because receiving the ready-made doesn't require search at all.

Search is needed only by someone who lives in deficit. A deficit of time, of information, of local causality. That's the normal mode of a creature with little data and many problems. But if there is a layer of reality on which the admissible configurations already coexist — then on that layer search doesn't work, not because it's hard, but because it isn't needed. Access to what is already given requires no production.

Here it's worth pinning down one distinction, because without it the whole construction slides apart. I distinguish two modes in which the new appears: production and access. Production is manufacturing: the system takes raw material, processes it by rules, outputs a result. Any step inside that process can in principle be called computation, however clever the processing. Access is access: the system receives a finished structure not as the result of its own work, but as something it turned out to be connected to. AGI, however powerful it may be, is by its architecture a production machine. It transforms one volume of data into another. A human, by my hypothesis, sometimes operates in access mode. Not always. Most of human life is production: we calculate, we search, we optimize. And there AGI beats us decisively. But at rare points the mode switches, and then something happens that production does not generate in principle.

From this formulation an important distinction follows immediately, and I'd better make it out loud, or everything will blur into a cloud.

The word "access" is a dangerous one. It makes the reader picture a library where ready-made answers are stored. Whoever is connected has pulled the right volume off the shelf. Whoever isn't sits in the dark with no library. That, of course, is not what I'm talking about. If insight were the retrieval of a ready-made answer from some external repository, it would be experienced as retrieval — recognizable, repeatable, finite in form. It is experienced differently. It is experienced as the emergence of something that did not exist before. And that "did not exist before" is meant literally, not rhetorically.

So a different metaphor is needed. Not a library metaphor. An engineering one.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *Yes, here I'd also like to tell you a certain metaphor, because this one is already getting a bit more engineering-flavored in some sense. Imagine we have some machine that consists of various parts. The parts are just lying there, unconnected. Each part is essentially a primitive with limited functionality. But if you connect all the parts, the machine emerges as a completely new entity.*
>
> *Access happens not in the computer sense, where you get access to some database you still have to pull things out of, but in the sense of access to production. Connections get established, and when the necessary number of connections is in place, that catastrophic change happens. It's like assembling a machine: you insert the last part, and that moment is a mathematical catastrophe.*
>
> *Before that event there was just a pile of spare parts. But after that event you get a new entity with functionality orders of magnitude — if not hundreds of orders of magnitude — higher than what existed before.*

This metaphor is not decoration and not a repetition of what I've already said. It is different in substance.

Picture a heap of metal on the garage floor. Gears, shafts, a housing, bearings, pistons, wires. Each part by itself does little. A piston can move up and down. A gear can spin. Taken separately — no machine at all. You can call this stockpile "potential," but that's mostly self-consolation. There is no engine, and there won't be one until someone assembles it.

Assembly is not the accumulation of parts. You can wait for assembly a long time: add one more piece of iron — then another — and still no machine. And suddenly, at some moment, a part gets inserted that closes the connection. Not the last one on the list, but the one that completes the critical configuration. And in that second, what lies on the garage floor stops being a heap of metal, and an engine appears. With new properties that none of the parts had on its own. A piston doesn't "know" how to deliver torque to a shaft. A gear doesn't "know" how to accelerate a car. But the engine knows. Not because it learned. Because it got assembled.

That moment is a catastrophe in the strict sense. Not a smooth improvement from a heap of metal to a so-so engine, but a jump: either the parts are lying there, or the machine is running. There is no in-between. And the functionality jumped not by percentage points but by orders of magnitude. Before assembly the garage couldn't drive anywhere. After assembly — it drives.

Connectedness, in this metaphor, is not access to a finished engine sitting somewhere in the cloud. It is access to couplability. To the capacity of specific elements to couple in such a way that a whole emerges that exceeds their sum. No file was downloaded. No database was queried. A phase assembly took place — and an entity appeared that had not existed before, either inside the system or outside it.

Carried over to insight, the picture changes qualitatively. Insight is not retrieval but assembly. Not the receipt of a ready-made solution, but the emergence of a configuration in which the solution turns out to be a built-in property of the new whole. That is why it arrives whole, and that is exactly why the explanation is always constructed after the fact: first the machine appeared, then the person figured out how it works.

And then connectedness is not a thread to a warehouse of answers. It is the capacity for assemblies that do not happen in the ordinary searchable space. The capacity to reach the critical configuration — not by computation, but by some other route that itself needs explaining. I'm not going to explain that route now. I'm only pointing to where, by my sense of it, it runs.

Here an objection is ripening in me, and I'll say it out loud so as not to pretend it isn't there. One could say: what kind of connectedness is this — it's ordinary complexity theory. The appearance of the new out of critical connectivity is emergence, a phenomenon in which the whole acquires properties that none of the parts had. Water is an emergent property of hydrogen and oxygen atoms. Life is an emergent property of intricately assembled organic chemistry. Consciousness is, possibly, an emergent property of an intricately assembled nervous system. No connectedness is needed here; everything happens locally. Take elements, push the connections to critical density, get a new whole.

It's a reasonable objection. And in many cases it works. I don't deny emergence as a phenomenon. The trouble is that emergence explains one part of the story — that a new whole appears when critical connectivity is reached — and doesn't explain the other part. Namely: where a local system gets the knowledge of which particular configuration of connections will yield a working whole. In the metaphor of the heap of spare parts this is especially clear. There are many parts in the garage. There are astronomically more possible ways to connect them. The overwhelming majority of connections produce junk: either it doesn't work at all, or it works badly. The machine is a very narrow subset of all possible configurations. Searching blindly, you wouldn't reach it in timespans comparable to the age of the universe. And people reach it. Not always, not everyone — but they reach it.

Emergence as a term describes the result; it doesn't explain the navigation. It says: when the coupling happens, the new appears. It doesn't say: why the coupling that happened was exactly this one. Connectedness, in my hypothesis, is an attempt to answer the second question. Not "where does the new whole come from" — emergence answers that. But "where does a local system get the ability to find exactly the configuration that makes the whole work." These are different questions, and they are easy to confuse.

This is all still a hypothesis. I'm not proving it. I don't have an AGI on hand to run an honest comparative experiment with, and I don't have a physical theory in which a wider layer of reality would be rigorously defined. I'm working with a conjecture that so far has only internal logical coherence and a few places where it explains what the closed computational picture explains poorly. That's not enough for a theory. It's enough for a conversation.

One practical touch — and I'll stop.

If a human really does operate as a non-closed system, this should have an observable consequence. Not in a lab — in life. There should be cases where a person with his back against the wall demonstrates capabilities he doesn't have and couldn't have in a calm state. And such cases are a banal part of human biography; we've all seen them, everyone has their own.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *When a person has a fire lit under his ass, he's capable of such astonishing things that I think for him artificial intelligence, a meteorite, a pandemic — they're all just accelerants of evolution. It's simply that, for the most part, most people are asleep, and they have no need to rev up their own capacity for adaptation like that. Over millions of years the human became an absolutely perfect adaptation machine, and so far, from my point of view, he has no equal.*

I would only soften "absolutely perfect" and "no equal" — that's overreach, and I can hear it myself. But the core stands: a human in comfort and a human under pressure are two different creatures in what they can do. In comfort he considers himself a predictable system and behaves like one. Under pressure he starts doing things that weren't present in his usual behavior even in embryo — and often can't explain afterward how exactly he did them.

If you look at this through the "closed computational system" frame, behavior under pressure looks like a riddle. The environment supplied no new data. The brain grew no new computational resources. Where did the thing that wasn't there before come from.

But if you look through the frame of non-closedness, the explanation surfaces on its own. Pressure is a mode in which a person stops operating as an offline device. Connections that stayed severed in comfort begin to couple. Configurations that never reached critical in calm now reach it. The machine that lay disassembled in the garage gets assembled — because otherwise you don't survive. And then an external shock is not a threat but an accelerator: it switches on, in a person, the assembly mode that is switched off at rest.

I am deliberately placing this next to the connected-device metaphor, not apart from it. These are not two different theses; it is the same thought from two sides. On one side — the human as a local interface whose power lies not in compute but in non-closedness. On the other — the human under pressure, whose assembly runs faster because access to couplability, normally dormant, switches on. Without pressure the interface runs in the background. Under pressure it goes to full power.

Can all of this be said without the words "wider reality" and "non-closed system"? It can, but not honestly. Because any attempt to avoid those words drags the conversation back into the frame of closed computation, and in that frame there is nothing to explain insight under insufficient context with. Better to keep the uncomfortable formulation than a comfortable one that doesn't work.

I realize that an attentive reader already has a question ripening here — and it's the right question. Connected — to what. And if a human is connected, why wouldn't AGI connect to the same thing. The rest of the conversation hangs on those questions. And that's where we're headed.
