# 2. One Ontology, Many Verticals: Life as Game and Drama

**Private document. Not for open publication.** This is the second of five essays in the Trajectories series, rewritten in a different language. In the first, I changed the atom of intelligence — from the answer to the trajectory — and assembled a core of three layers: a reality model, a scenario space, and a positioning layer. Here I stress-test that core, running it through several different spheres of life. But this time the language is not my own. I take two ready-made, codified professional vocabularies — game design and dramaturgy — and show that they describe the same ontology of navigation, only in the unified language of entire disciplines rather than in my private picture.

## 0. TL;DR — one engine, different settings <a id="tldr"></a>

In the first essay I changed the atom of intelligence: the unit is not an inference but a trajectory, and above it stands a core of three layers — a reality model (active and reactive elements), a scenario space, and a positioning layer. Here I advance a thesis that would sound abstract without that core — and becomes testable with it.

The thesis is simple, and I lay it on the table at once. Take any complex sphere of life — career, business, war, the state, medicine, education, family — and look closely at how it is built. In each you will find one and the same grammar: a world with rules that define what is possible at all; conflict and probabilistic forces the agent does not control; the agent itself, moving through that field; a space of possible playthroughs; an arc of movement and its vector; a repeating cycle; outcomes — failure, victory, reward; and stakes rising toward the resolution. This is not my invention. Precisely this grammar has already been formalized by two mature disciplines. Game design described *how movement works* in a world with rules, goals, and outcomes. Dramaturgy described *what the movement is for and what it changes in the agent*. Together they give the ontology of navigation a ready-made, generally accepted vocabulary — which is why I take them instead of inventing my own.

I prove the thesis by a run-through. I take the three layers of the core, rename them into the terms of these two fields — and lay them over several verticals in succession. Career reads as the selection of a strategy in a *possibility space*. War speaks this language outright, literally: theater, campaign, three levels of action. Medicine has already laid down its *clinical pathways* — scenarios with obstacles, stakes, and outcomes. Education is a progression through levels with rising stakes. In each I find the same game engine and the same dramatic arc, and only the setting changes. And when the verticals line up in a row, the engineering prejudice I want to expose becomes visible: the belief that every sphere needs its own separate AI. It does not. What is needed is one engine and many settings on top of it.

## 1. The grammar of a complex sphere and two languages for it <a id="1-grammar"></a>

In the first essay the core assembled into a vertical stack of three layers. At the bottom, a reality model built of active and reactive elements: some generate impact, others change state in response. Above it, a scenario space: a set of typical trajectories over that model. On top, a positioning layer that chooses which train to board and what vector to follow. I will not re-explain these layers; they are fixed, and the series stands on them. I take them as given and ask the question that follows from them directly.

If the core is invariant, why do the people who use it look like members of incompatible professions? A career coach, a business strategist, a military planner, an attending physician, an education methodologist — between them, it seems, lies an abyss. Different languages, different tools, different diplomas. Common sense says these are different intelligences, and the engineering instinct obediently answers in kind — to each sphere its own special engine, its own model, its own team. I hold that this is an illusion, and I want to dispel it at once rather than save it for the finale. All these people are doing one and the same thing. They guide an agent through a world with rules toward the desired outcome, choosing a course in the space of possible playthroughs. What differs is not their intelligence but their setting.

And here begins the central move of this variant. I claim that every complex sphere of life has one and the same grammar, and that this grammar does not need to be invented, because it has already been codified. Let me lay it out node by node. There is the **game world** (a constructed space, defined by rules and setting)[^g1] — the field on which everything happens. There are **rules** — formal constraints that do not act on their own but set the boundaries of the possible; the game-design canon says this almost word for word: rules define the space within which interaction is possible[^g2]. Before us are the passive constraints of Essay 1, named with the discipline's own term; and the very coupling "real rules plus fictional world" — rules-as-constraints over a setting — is spelled out in game design directly[^g7]. There is the **agent** (*player/agent* — the one acting within the rules)[^g1], and there is its **mechanic** — the method by which the agent acts upon the state of the world: in the academic definition, mechanics are "methods invoked by agents for interacting with the game state"[^g2], an almost literal formula of "an active element acts upon a reactive one." There is the **possibility space** (the space of all admissible actions, which the player explores)[^g3] — a direct analogue of the scenario space. There is the **core loop** (the repeatable base cycle "impact → response → signal → next impact")[^g8], the **challenge/obstacle**[^g4], **score and metrics**[^g3], and there are the **outcomes**: the **win condition**[^g3], the **failure state**[^g6], and the **reward**[^g1] that closes the loop. All of this is the vocabulary of a single discipline, game design, whose very business is describing navigation through a world with rules, goals, stakes, and outcomes.

Game design answers the question "how movement works." But movement has a second question — "what it is for and what it changes in the one who moves." Dramaturgy answers that question, and its terms map onto the upper, semantic layer. The agent's trajectory through time is the **arc** (*character arc* — the psychological transformation the agent undergoes along the way: they leave the story different from how they entered it)[^d3]. The mechanics give progression — levels accumulated, resources gathered; the arc gives meaning — the agent has become someone else. The risk mode of a position is the **stakes** (what the agent loses or wins depending on the outcome; the price of the resolution)[^d1]. The point where the trajectory qualitatively changes direction is the **turning point** (in Aristotle — peripeteia: the reversal by which the action turns into its opposite)[^d1][^d2]. The growth of the price of a wrong course along the path is **escalation** (*rising stakes* — the conflict intensifies, the price of error grows toward the resolution)[^d1]. And the deepest foundation goes back to Aristotle: a story is the structure of **action** in time (*mythos*, *praxis*), not a set of lines of dialogue[^d2]. Here is the root support beneath the very change of atom in Essay 1 — the trajectory, not the inference: dramaturgy has been speaking about action-in-time from the very beginning, for two and a half thousand years.

From here on I keep one discipline of hygiene and ask the reader to keep it too. For every node of the grammar I take exactly one term from one field — one term per node. Game design carries the mechanics of navigation: world, rules, cycle, move versus strategy, score, outcome. Dramaturgy carries the meaning of movement: arc-as-transformation, stakes, turning point, escalation. Where both fields offer a word for the same node — and they often do — I take the word of the owning field and do not glue on the second as "one more synonym." This is not pedantry. It is a direct consequence of the same principle of economy for which I undertook this variant in the first place: one word per thought.

And at once an honest asymmetry between the two fields, so that later I do not pass off wish for fact. The load-bearing language of the run-through is game design: its mechanics of navigation work on all the verticals without exception. Dramaturgy is not an equal second language but a targeted amplifier: arc, stakes, and turning point switch on where a transforming subject stands at the center — career, medicine, education, family. In war, business, the state, the drama layer carries almost no work: "the arc of a territory" or "the arc of a product" would be a stretch, and I do not force them. Where drama is empty, mechanics alone speaks.

So that the run-through does not look like renaming for renaming's sake, let me mark at once three places where the new language carries real understanding rather than cosmetics. First: the *possibility space* is an independently built academic construct of an entire discipline[^g3], and it proves that "the set of admissible trajectories defined by the rules" is not my invention but a recognized frame. Second, and central: the pair **move versus strategy** (*move vs strategy* — a single move within the rules versus a plan for distributing moves across the whole game for the sake of victory)[^g3] is, word for word, the "local step versus productive vector" of the first essay — only game theory formalized this pair long before me. Third: the locking link, MDA (*mechanics → dynamics → aesthetics* — rules generate the system's behavior, behavior leads to the target outcome)[^g5], maps onto the triple "active elements → emergent behavior → target outcome." Here I make a caveat at once, without hiding the seam in a footnote: the third member of the triad, *aesthetics*, is in the original about the player's emotional response, and I take it not literally but as "target outcome/state" on non-life verticals. With that caveat, the link licenses the main claim: the verticals are not different games with different engines but one engine with different settings.

## 2. Why this language is legitimate and a private metaphor is not <a id="2-legitimacy"></a>

Here I owe an explanation, because in the first version of this essay I did exactly the opposite and was wrong. There I took a maritime picture — islands, winds, ships — and unfolded it into a whole vocabulary: gave it sections, introduced sub-terms, structured the entire text with it. That was a mistake. The maritime metaphor is my personal, invented illustration. To unfold it into a parallel language is to build a second layer of names on top of already defined concepts without adding a gram of understanding. A superfluous entity, a fifth wheel. Game design and dramaturgy are a different matter in substance, not in taste. They are existing, codified professional languages of entire disciplines, with canonical definitions in textbooks and peer-reviewed articles, which took shape without me and before me — precisely in order to describe the movement of an agent through a world with rules, stakes, and outcomes. To take them is to adapt a ready-made standard, not to breed a private picture. The standard already carries the work: it is unified, checkable, and behind every term stands a discipline. That is why the language is legitimate here and was not legitimate there.

But this legitimacy has a hard boundary, and I hold it. The right to take someone else's standard is not an indulgence to paint pseudo-terms in game-design colors. I take only the real, established words of these fields in their accepted meaning. Where a discipline has no canonical term, I do not invent one; I return to the concepts of the first essay and speak descriptively. Let me mark two such places honestly. For the "probabilistic forces" of Essay 1 — impersonal statistical currents such as the market, trends, the course of a disease — these fields have no exact canonical term: the dramaturgical "forces of antagonism" are the opposition of another's will, not statistics, and to substitute one for the other would distort both. And for the "positioning layer" there is no single word either; the nearest is the function of strategy, but that is not a layer. In both cases I do not glue on a false label; I speak directly in the concepts of the first essay. A borrowed language is legitimate exactly where it is real and where it fits; beyond that — my own terms.

## 3. A run through the verticals: same engine, different setting <a id="3-verticals"></a>

I begin with career, because its structure is the most recognizable. The **rules** here are structural: position levels, grades, formal requirements, regulations, industry-level and personal boundaries such as education or geography. They are hard: a grade cannot be leapt over by wishing, and this sets the geometry of any route. The probabilistic forces are trends inside the company and in the market: which functions are growing, who has just arrived as the new executive and reshuffled the alignment. The agent is a particular person with their skills, reputation, relationships. And the difference between the two levels of the game is immediately visible. An assistant that edits résumés works at the level of the **move** — a single mechanic within the rules. What is needed is a **strategy** — a plan for distributing moves across the whole game for the sake of the win condition: hold the map of positions and trends, unfold the *branching* (choices open and close future branches) of routes — vertical growth within a function, a lateral move into an adjacent one, a jump across companies to a grade higher, an exploratory move such as an internship — and steer toward the target role, even if individual moves along the way will be imperfect. This is the same pair "move versus strategy" that carries the entire first essay, only in the career setting.

Career has a continuation worth pausing over. The same positioning layer turns one hundred eighty degrees and serves not the player but the one who assembles the game. From the person's side, it builds a personal possibility space — a map of career playthroughs. From the corporation's side, it matches people to projects. A project here is a coherent setting with its own level of stakes and its own forecast of forces: an M&A deal, the launch of a new market, a major transformation. And people are matched to it not by abstract skills but by arc type. One person loves going into uncharted territory and coming back with a result — that one goes to scout the new market. Another reliably holds predictability — that one scales what already works. The task is not to fill a slot but to place agents across settings so that every project receives the arc type it needs and every person ends up where it advances their own playthrough. Career coaching and talent management cease to be two different tasks; they are one possibility space over one model, in which the system simultaneously guides people and staffs projects. And to work this way it does not need to be an insider: it suffices to see the actual game — moves and outcomes, not résumés. This fork is not hypothetical: internal *talent marketplaces* (systems that match employee profiles to internal opportunities) are an actually deployed, though still maturing, corporate practice[^v6][^v7].

War raises the stakes and speaks this language literally — here it is native. Military doctrine long ago formalized what in the first essay I called the difference between move and strategy: war is distributed across three levels — strategy, operational art, tactics. Strategy sets the goals; operational art orchestrates campaigns within a theater; tactics resolves individual engagements; the intermediate, operational level is the link connecting strategic goals with the tactical employment of forces[^v1][^v2]. The translation into the grammar is direct: the theater of operations is the rules and the setting, the adversary and the overall situation are the probabilistic forces and the conflict, operations and special missions are agents, each dispatched to its own sector of the field with its own geometry and its own objectives. The word "level" I deliberately reserve here for military doctrine — strategy, operational art, tactics — and not for game design, so that one and the same term does not carry two different meanings within a single paragraph. A special operation — a raid, an airborne assault, an act of sabotage — is an agent of a special class for a special sector: ultra-precise selection of the team, deep reconnaissance of the situation, hard limits on time and noise, a one-time window, a high price of failure. You cannot send a random person there; what goes there is a class honed for that terrain — the same placement of agents across settings as in career, only the stakes are different. And the main lesson of war for my engine is that the **win condition** is reached not by the one who conducts every engagement impeccably but by the one who correctly distributes forces across directions and sustains the campaign's logistics. Perfect tactics in a war lost at the level of strategy is a polished move in a game lost at the level of strategy. The same two levels of the game, the same primacy of geometry over the quality of an individual move — only the price of error is measured differently. War is the place where the difference between move and strategy is not a metaphor but a doctrinal norm.

Medicine carries the same grammar into a setting where the probabilistic forces are no longer a market but biology, and the stakes are maximal. The rules here are harder than anywhere else: anatomy and physiology, protocols, contraindications, available technologies, regulation, the patient's chronic conditions, age, genetics — everything that sets the shape of the situation and does not change quickly. The probabilistic forces are the course of the disease, infections, responses to therapy, lifestyle, random events. The agent is the patient together with the treating team, moving along a joint trajectory through diagnosis, choice of therapy, correction, rehabilitation. Here I deliberately do not take the game term *playthrough*: a game can be replayed, a disease cannot, and to impose on biology the teleology of "content made to be played through" would be a lie of vocabulary. Where the outcome is reversible, the game language is precise; where it is irreversible, I return to the concepts of the first essay. And medicine has already partly formalized this trajectory explicitly. A clinical pathway (a multidisciplinary route for managing a patient with a predictable course, in which interventions are ordered in time) is a course plotted in advance according to protocols, and systematic reviews show that it works precisely where the course is predictable, ensuring the timeliness of interventions without harm[^v3][^v4]. A clinical pathway is the written-out core loop of the vertical: the cycle "intervention → the organism's response → signal → next intervention." And the mechanics here come in different classes — by where the intervention strikes. The Russian medical tradition divides therapy into etiotropic (aimed at the cause of the disease), pathogenetic (aimed at the mechanism of its development), and symptomatic (aimed at its manifestations)[^v5]; the English-language literature classifies differently — and what matters to me is not which partition is more correct but that the positioning layer here, too, chooses which mechanic to act with: reconnoiter the cause, press on the mechanism, or suppress the symptom. And over the mechanics lies a semantic layer the protocol does not contain: positioning chooses not a single pill but an entire risk mode — go in aggressively, hold a sparing tactic, or set the course toward quality of life when no good outcome remains. Here I depart from the game vocabulary deliberately: to call death a *failure state* and palliative care a "stakes mode" would be to borrow from the game a reversibility this vertical does not have. The stakes here are ultimate precisely because there is no replaying; this is a concept of the first essay, not a term of the field. Nor is the matter exhausted by the clinical pathway: living navigation observes the patient's long arc, learns from the outcomes of previous cases, and proposes routes not only by diagnosis but by the vector of the person's life, for whom one and the same diagnosis is a different setting.

Education closes out the vivid series and shows progression and stakes in their purest form. The rules are standards, curricula, age limits, basic cognitive regularities: what cannot be shifted within a semester. The probabilistic forces are demand for professions, technological shifts, socioeconomic trends, the student's personal environment. The agent is a particular person or group on its own **arc** of competence development. And here the engine is visible without any translation: a course of study is a written-out **progression** (a structure of buildup: the accumulation of capabilities and difficulty along the way)[^g9] through levels with **escalating stakes** — the price of a wrong vector grows toward graduation, because the further one goes, the harder it is to retrain. And the education system itself likewise unfolds as a **branching** of possible development paths — from a return to the former model to radical renewal: education researchers distinguish several types of such scenarios, ranging roughly from the restorationist to the innovative, and argue over one thing only — which course to choose. Before us is the question of positioning in its pure form: not "deliver the right lesson" but choose a vector of progression. An educational assistant that does not understand this remains yet another course platform — it polishes the individual lesson, a single move, and does not see the arc along which the student is moving. The positioning layer, by contrast, sees where the person stands in the possibility space and which course leads them toward the target settings — career, enterprise, health, civic role; and the win condition here is not "learn a fact" but to be on a playthrough that converges, over the long run, with where the person needs to arrive.

Business, the state, and the family complete the series, and I give them briefly — not because the engine is weaker there, but because after four full unfoldings it reads at half a word. In business, the rules are market structure, regulation, resource and technological frames; the forces are demand, competitors, channels, trends of attention; the agents are products, initiatives, campaigns; and the funnel is the chosen strategy along which the positioning layer steers a *portfolio of playthroughs*, extinguishing some branches and scaling others by score. An individual conversion here is a single move; the niche, the audience, and the monetization model are the strategy of the game, and a perfectly optimized move in a game lost at the level of the niche saves nothing. State and municipal governance is the same engine in a larger setting: the rules here are the constitution, laws, budget rules, levels of government, and the territories themselves; the forces are economic cycles, demography, migration, political and external shifts that the authorities do not control but are obliged to build into their scenarios; the agents are national projects, reforms, municipal initiatives, and the teams that carry them. A project unfolds into a set of levels of a single territory — transport, utilities, education, public safety — across which a portfolio of programs is steered in concert; and the positioning layer once again chooses not the right move on one question but a vector of development for the territory under the given rules and forces. In the family, the rules are characters, values, the histories of the parental families, socioeconomic frames; the forces are crises, relocations, the birth of children, age transitions that cannot be predicted but whose patterns are visible; the agent is the family as a single subject moving along a long arc. And navigation here is occupied with the same thing as everywhere else: not untangling a single quarrel — a single step — but holding the vector of the arc: where the couple is moving, toward closeness or toward estrangement, and whether the turning point has come to change course. Seven settings, from grades to marriage, from the theater of war to the clinical protocol. The rules differ everywhere, the forces are each sphere's own, the agents are of every class. The engine is one.

## 4. Different settings, not different engines <a id="4-conclusion"></a>

When the verticals are lined up in a row, what I placed on the first page comes through in them — and now I can rest it on the run-through. In all seven spheres, one engine is at work. The game world with its rules, the possibility space over it, the positioning layer on top, the core loop inside — this game order did not waver in career, in war, in medicine, in education, or in the governance of a territory. The dramatic layer — arc and stakes as the meaning of movement — did not lie evenly everywhere, but only where a transforming subject stands at the center; in war and governance the work is carried by mechanics alone, and I hold to that honestly, without forcing an "arc of a territory." Only the setting changed: what counts as a rule, which forces act, which agents move across the field. Grades and contraindications are rules of different natures. The labor market and the course of a disease are forces of different natures. An employee, a special-operations team, a patient, a student are agents of one engine. The game itself — unfold the branching of playthroughs and choose a strategy toward the win condition — changed nowhere. And this is no stretch for beauty's sake: game design and dramaturgy lay over all seven precisely because they are, from the very start, the unified languages of navigation through a world with rules, stakes, and outcomes. They have already done the work of generalization that I am repeating on the material of life. Game design derived its vocabulary not from one game but from thousands of different ones — strategy games, role-playing games, racing games, puzzles — and its terms are therefore invariant to setting by construction: a rule remains a rule whether we speak of a chessboard or of the labor market. Dramaturgy did the same with stories — from ancient tragedy to the television series — and derived arc, stakes, and turning point as the form of any action-in-time, indifferent to its material. I have merely added to their list of settings seven more, taken not from fiction but from life.

This allows me to name the engineering prejudice against which this entire essay is written. Common sense and the industry say in one voice: to each sphere its own special AI. A separate engine for recruiting, a separate one for military planning, a separate medical one, a separate educational one, each with its own model, its own team, its own jargon. I contend that this is an error at the level of the atom. These are not different engines. They are different settings for one engine. The specificity of a vertical lives not in the core but in the setting — in the set of rules, forces, and agents loaded into that vertical. To build a separate engine for each sphere is to multiply the core where only the setting should be multiplied. The correct architecture is the inverse: one maximally abstract engine concerned with rules, the possibility space, move-versus-strategy, and active/reactive elements — and verticals as interchangeable settings on top of it.

From this follows the bet I am placing on the core. It must be designed not for the first vertical one takes into work but for the invariant — so that adding a new sphere is the loading of a new setting, not the rewriting of the engine. Career, business, war, the state, medicine, education, family are not seven products that will one day have to be integrated somehow. They are seven settings of one game, and it remains common by construction, because what was common was the engine through which they were all described. The concepts of the first essay — reality model, scenario space, positioning layer, productive vector — exist precisely for this: they set a single format. The language of game design and dramaturgy I took not in place of them but on top of them — as a universal upper vocabulary proving that this format is not my private whim but a recognized grammar of navigation.

And here the engine itself suggests the next move. All along I have spoken of verticals as if they were different games played by different agents. But the human being is one. The same person simultaneously builds a career, runs an enterprise, undergoes treatment, studies, lives in a family — and these are not seven separate agents in seven worlds but a single point whose position must be measured along all the axes at once. If the engine is one, then the person, too, ceases to be a set of isolated games and becomes a single figure in a multidimensional space through which they move along all dimensions at once. What that space is, how its axes are defined, and where their combined movement carries a person — that is where the next essay begins.

## Glossary <a id="glossary"></a>

This variant takes two ready-made professional languages — game design and dramaturgy — and lays them over the ontology of navigation of the first essay. The glossary is therefore arranged in three blocks: game-design terms (the mechanics of navigation, "how movement works"), dramaturgy terms (the meaning of movement, "what it is for and what it changes in the agent"), and the load-bearing concepts of the first essay (the formal lower layer that these two languages describe from above). Each entry for a game/drama term gives the field's accepted definition and, with the arrow `↔`, its correspondence to an Essay 1 concept; the Essay 1 concepts are given verbatim from the series registry (`source/траектории/CONCEPTS.md`) and are not redefined. The order follows the logic of the argument, not the alphabet. Two nodes of the ontology — the probabilistic forces and the positioning layer — are deliberately left without a field term: these disciplines have no exact canonical word for them, and they are held directly in the concepts of Essay 1 (see Section 2).

### Game-design terms (the mechanics of navigation)

**Game world** — *an established game-design term.* A constructed space, defined by rules and setting, on which the action takes place[^g1]. ↔ corresponds to the **reality model** of Essay 1 (the bottom layer of the core, generating the scenario space).

**Rules** — *an established game-design term.* Formal constraints that do not act on their own but set the boundaries of the possible: rules define the space within which interaction is possible[^g2]; the coupling "real rules plus fictional world" is spelled out directly in the canon[^g7]. ↔ correspond to the **active/reactive elements** of Essay 1 in their reactive, slow part — the **passive constraints** that set the geometry of a situation.

**Setting** — *an established term of game design and dramaturgy (shared).* The concrete filling of a world on top of invariant rules. ↔ corresponds to the **domain specifics** of Essay 1 (the particular set of constraints, forces, and agents that distinguishes one vertical from another under one core).

**Mechanic** — *an established game-design term.* The method by which an agent acts upon the state of the world: "methods invoked by agents for interacting with the game state"[^g2]. ↔ corresponds to the **active element** of Essay 1 (that which generates impact upon a reactive element).

**Possibility space** — *an established game-design term.* The space of all admissible actions, which the player explores[^g3]. ↔ a direct analogue of the **scenario space** of Essay 1 (the set of typical trajectories over the reality model, across which a course is plotted). An independently built academic construct of an entire discipline — one of the variant's three load-bearing bridges.

**Move vs strategy** — *an established term of game design and game theory.* A single move within the rules versus a plan for distributing moves across the whole game for the sake of the win condition[^g3]. ↔ word for word, the pair **local step versus productive vector** of Essay 1 (the individual inference/step versus the direction of movement, which is what is managed instead of the individual answer). The variant's central bridge.

**Branching** — *an established game-design term.* A structure in which choices open and close future branches of a playthrough. ↔ corresponds to the **fan of scenarios** of Essay 1 (the set of trajectories available from the current point).

**Core loop** — *an established term of game-design practice.* The repeatable base cycle "impact → response → signal → next impact"[^g8]. ↔ corresponds to the mechanics of the **scenario** of Essay 1 (the chain of steps "active impact → reactive change" with goals and metrics); the term "scenario" itself remains with the Essay 1 canon and is not redefined.

**Progression** — *an established game-design term.* A structure of buildup: the accumulation of capabilities and difficulty along the way[^g9], in contrast to emergence. ↔ corresponds to the mechanical side of the **drift** of Essay 1 (the accumulation of shift along the path); the semantic side of the same movement is carried by the drama term "arc."

**Playthrough** — *an established game-design term.* One realized game from entry to outcome[^g7]. ↔ corresponds to the **trajectory** of Essay 1 in its formal-mechanical aspect (the realized path of an agent). Deliberately not applied in medicine: a game can be replayed, a disease cannot (see Section 3).

**Challenge / obstacle** — *an established game-design term.* The unit of gameplay, lying "at the heart" of the game[^g4]. ↔ corresponds to a **local obstacle** on the trajectory of Essay 1 (that which the positioning layer goes around or through when choosing a course).

**Score / metrics** — *an established game-design term.* A measurable signal of the state of the game[^g3]. ↔ corresponds to that by which, in Essay 1, the system answers the question of the vector — **"which train you are riding" and whether it is going the right way** (the productive-vector metaphor, owned by Essay 1).

**Win condition** — *an established game-design term.* The target state whose attainment ends the game in victory[^g3]. ↔ corresponds to the target point of the **productive vector** of Essay 1 (where the chosen direction of movement leads).

**Failure state** — *an established game-design term.* The state of a lost game or branch[^g6]. ↔ corresponds to the branch of the **fan of scenarios** of Essay 1 that the positioning layer strives to avoid. Deliberately not applied on irreversible verticals (death in medicine) — there the Essay 1 concept of "stakes" does the work, not the game term (see Section 3).

**Reward** — *an established game-design term.* A signal of advancement that closes the loop[^g1]. ↔ corresponds to the signal feeding the **memory and insight layer** of Essay 1 (that which accumulates between steps and episodes).

**The MDA triad (mechanics → dynamics → aesthetics)** — *an established game-design framework.* Rules generate the system's behavior (dynamics); behavior leads to the target outcome[^g5]. ↔ maps onto the Essay 1 triple **active elements → emergent behavior → target outcome**. The variant's caveat: the third member, *aesthetics*, is in the original about the player's emotional response; here it is taken as "target outcome/state" on non-life verticals, not literally (see Section 1). One of the variant's three load-bearing bridges.

### Dramaturgy terms (the meaning of movement)

**Arc (character arc)** — *an established term of dramaturgy and narratology.* The psychological transformation an agent undergoes over the course of a story: they leave it different from how they entered it[^d3]. ↔ corresponds to the **trajectory** of Essay 1 in its semantic aspect — the formulation "an episode of an organism's life," from which the organism emerges different. The drama contribution on top of mechanical progression: progression gives accumulation, the arc gives the meaning of the change.

**Stakes** — *an established dramaturgy term.* What the agent loses or wins depending on the outcome; the price of the resolution[^d1]. ↔ corresponds to the **risk mode** within the **position** of Essay 1 (what the risk mode is in the system's current situation). Explains in one term why "the price of error in war ≠ in career" — these are different stakes of one navigation, not different navigations.

**Turning point (in Aristotle — peripeteia)** — *an established dramaturgy term.* The point where the action qualitatively changes direction, turning into its opposite[^d1][^d2]. ↔ corresponds to the moment of changing the **productive vector** of Essay 1 — the decision to change "trains" (the vector metaphor, owned by Essay 1).

**Escalation (rising stakes)** — *an established dramaturgy term.* The growth of the price of a wrong course along the path: the conflict intensifies, the price of error grows toward the resolution[^d1]. ↔ corresponds to the semantic side of the **drift** of Essay 1 (the growth of shift along the trajectory); the mechanical side of the same is carried by the game term "progression."

**Action-in-time (mythos, praxis; Aristotle)** — *an established dramaturgy concept.* A story is the structure of action in time, not a set of lines of dialogue[^d2]. ↔ the root support beneath the very change of atom in Essay 1 — **the trajectory, not the inference**: dramaturgy has described action-in-time from the very beginning. Used once, as a foundation; not developed further.

### Load-bearing concepts of Essay 1 (the formal lower layer)

Given verbatim from the series registry (`source/траектории/CONCEPTS.md`); this variant does not redefine them but shows that the two external languages describe them from above.

**Trajectory** — a stretch of time over which an agent plans, acts, observes, corrects course, and accumulates experience; the atom of intelligence in place of the inference. The game aspect — *playthrough*; the drama aspect — *arc*.

**Reality model (active / reactive elements)** — the bottom layer of the core: active elements generate impact, reactive ones change state in response. Game language: the *game world* (rules + setting), where the *mechanic* is the active element.

**Scenario space** — the set of typical trajectories over the reality model; that across which a course is plotted. Game analogue — the *possibility space*.

**Positioning layer** — the top layer of the core: it chooses which train to board and what vector to follow. **Deliberately without a field term:** neither game design nor dramaturgy offers a single canonical word for "the layer that chooses position"; the nearest is the function of strategy, but that is not a layer. Held directly as an Essay 1 concept (see Section 2).

**Productive vector** — the direction of movement in the scenario space; the object of control in place of the individual step. The game mechanic of direction — *strategy*; the target point — the *win condition*.

**Probabilistic forces** — active, alien forces caught by statistics rather than by control (the market, trends, the course of a disease). **Deliberately without a field term:** the dramaturgical "forces of antagonism" are the opposition of another's will, not impersonal statistics, and substituting one for the other would distort both. Held directly as an Essay 1 concept (see Section 2).

**Local step / deviation** — an individual step, noise along the line; less important than the vector. Game analogue — the *move* in the pair "move vs strategy."

**Drift** — the shift of a trajectory under the action of steps and vector; the geometry of movement matters more than the quality of a step. The game side — *progression*; the drama side — *escalation*.

**Vertical** — a sphere of complex life (career, business, war, the state, medicine, education, family): not a separate intelligence with its own engine, but the same Essay 1 ontology with particular rules, forces, and agents. In this variant — a *setting* on top of one engine.

### External concepts drawn upon (verticals)

**Levels of war: strategy, operational art, tactics** — *drawn from outside (military doctrine).* A formalized division of action into three scales: strategy sets the goals, operational art orchestrates campaigns, tactics resolves individual engagements[^v1][^v2]. In the text, the word "level" is deliberately reserved for military doctrine, not for game design. Confirms that the productive vector matters more than the quality of an individual move.

**Clinical pathway** — *drawn from outside (medicine).* A multidisciplinary route for managing a patient with a predictable course, in which interventions are ordered in time[^v3][^v4]. In the text — the written-out *core loop* of the medical vertical and an example of already formalized navigation.

**The therapy triad: etiotropic / pathogenetic / symptomatic** — *drawn from outside (the Russian medical tradition).* The division of therapy by point of application: cause / mechanism / manifestation[^v5]; the English-language literature classifies differently. In the text — the choice of the *mechanic* of intervention to fit the specifics of a case.

**Internal talent marketplaces** — *drawn from outside (HR practice).* Systems that match employee profiles to internal opportunities; an actually deployed, though still maturing, practice[^v6][^v7]. In the text — empirical confirmation of the fork "the navigator serves not the player but the one who assembles the game."
