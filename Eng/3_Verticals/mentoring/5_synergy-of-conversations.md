## TL;DR <a id="tldr"></a>

I have long noticed an effect that shows up in far from every conversation: after meeting a certain kind of interlocutor, new ideas keep coming to me for several more days. Not during the meeting itself, but afterward—in the shower, behind the wheel, on a walk. And it is not a retelling of what I heard: a week later, I often barely remember what exactly we talked about. What remains is something else—a changed trajectory of my own thinking.

For a long time this picture seemed poorly explainable to me, until I put together three separate lines of research: the **incubation effect** in cognitive psychology (a thought you let go of continues to be processed in the background), **weak ties** and **structural holes** in the sociology of networks (novelty comes not from those close to you but from those who belong to a different cluster), and serendipity research (accidental discoveries do not happen by accident—they have structural conditions). All three explain the same phenomenon from different angles.

The effect works rarely. It requires three conditions at once: the interlocutor thinks differently, their competence is sufficiently high, and the conversation is exploratory rather than competitive. Absent any one of the three, it is an ordinary conversation after which nothing happens.

In a world where the bulk of professional communication has turned into performance, such conversations have become a scarce resource. Conversational AI covers some of the conditions, but not all. The single main practical takeaway: the quality of a conversation cannot be judged in the first hours after it. If nothing has shifted within three or four days, the effect was not there.

## 1. The Phenomenon: What I Notice <a id="1-phenomenon"></a>

I noticed long ago an effect that shows up with some people and not with others. After certain conversations—one-on-one or in a small group—thoughts and ideas keep arriving for several more days, ideas that most likely would not have appeared without that meeting. Not during the conversation itself, but afterward, at a distance of hours or days.

It is not every conversation. With most people it does not happen at all: we talk, we part, I forget. With some, it happens regularly. It has happened to me many times in my life; I stopped considering it a coincidence quite a while ago.

One could say that in such conversations I am given some especially useful piece of advice that I then mull over. But that is not the case. A week later I often barely remember the specific content—which topics we discussed, who said what. What remains is something else: a changed trajectory of my own thinking. I begin to look at my projects from an angle I had not looked from before. Moves appear that I would not have arrived at on my own. Sometimes—literally new hypotheses about what I am doing and why.

To this is added another, closely related effect known as **serendipity**—the unintended find. You search for one thing and discover something entirely different and unexpected. For me it happens not even in the conversation itself but in the process of formulating a reply to my interlocutor: while I am writing or speaking, I begin to see my own ideas from a different angle. The very act of explaining becomes an instrument of thinking, and in it surfaces something that was not in my head before.

For a long time I wanted to call this mysticism and leave it at that. But I had seen the effect in my own biography too often to write it off as chance. Rather, it is an example of what I privately call **potential synergy**: a situation in which one plus one is greater than two, and the difference cannot be reduced either to my contribution or to my interlocutor's taken separately. It arises in between.

Pragmatic logic works poorly here. This is not an exchange of information in the logic of productivity—"I give you five useful facts, you give me five." It is something else, and it is not very measurable in the moment. The benefit is there, it is real, and I see it after the fact in my projects. But at the moment of the conversation it is often not visible at all—which is exactly why such conversations are easy to devalue and bury under a more "useful" calendar.

## 2. Three Explanations from the Research <a id="2-explanations"></a>

When I started digging, it turned out the phenomenon is neither unique nor mystical. It has at least three independent explanations from different disciplines. They do not contradict one another—rather, they describe different facets of the same thing.

**First: cognitive activation and incubation.** A smart interlocutor does not do what it looks like from the outside. From the outside, it looks as if they are transmitting information to me. In reality, they are rearranging the map of associations in my head—connections between concepts I already had begin to be laid out differently. After the conversation ends, the brain keeps working in the background. This is the classic **incubation effect**—a stage from the four-step model of the creative process that Graham Wallas described back in 1926: preparation, incubation, illumination, verification[^1]. The idea of incubation—that the mind, having stepped away from a problem, continues to process it on its own and delivers a result hours or days later—has proved robust to a century of testing.

The most cited modern meta-analysis—Sio & Ormerod (2009)—showed that the effect is statistically significant: a break from working on a problem improves the solution, especially for divergent thinking—tasks involving divergent, associative search—rather than for linear insight problems with a single correct answer[^2]. An important detail: the effect works better when the preparation phase was long and the break itself is filled with something cognitively light that does not crowd out the background process. A parallel line—Dijksterhuis & Meurs (2006) on unconscious thought theory—showed that after receiving a complex prompt, people who were distracted by another task produce more original ideas than those who sat down to think right away[^3]. Their conclusion: conscious thought is convergent and focused; unconscious thought is associative and divergent.

The phrase "my best ideas come once I've stopped thinking about it" is not a metaphor. It is a measured cognitive mode. A conversation with an intelligent person triggers it especially strongly, because it tosses new nodes into the associative network.

**Second: weak ties and structural holes.** This is no longer psychology but the sociology of networks. In 1973, Mark Granovetter demonstrated something counterintuitive: people obtain uniquely new information not from close friends but from weak ties—acquaintances, acquaintances of acquaintances, former colleagues[^4]. The logic is simple. The close circle is closed: everyone knows everyone, and the same information and views circulate within it. A weak tie is a bridge into another cluster, and through it comes what simply does not exist inside your own.

Ronald Burt developed this line into the concept of **structural holes**—structural gaps between groups holding different information[^5]. A person who ends up between two such groups gains access to combinations unavailable within either one separately. This gives a structural explanation of why a conversation with someone "not of your circle" produces ideas that were not in my head: I did not stew in the same environment as my interlocutor, did not read the same books, did not face the same problems. When we talk, the structural hole between us collapses for a short time.

A later empirical refinement—Aral & Van Alstyne (2011)—added an important nuance: there is a trade-off between network diversity and channel bandwidth[^6]. Novelty comes not from just any weak tie but from one whose knowledge is deep and does not refresh too quickly. Otherwise the bridge is there, but there is nothing to carry across it.

**Third: serendipity.** The word has become fashionable, but behind it stands a serious research lineage. Robert Merton—one of the founders of the sociology of science—collected material on the subject from the 1950s onward; the book *The Travels and Adventures of Serendipity* came out only in 2004, after nearly half a century of work[^7]. Its central thesis: serendipity is not random, in the sense that it requires specific social structures that bring together people and ideas from different clusters. The laboratory, the university café, the regular informal conversation—these are the infrastructure of serendipity, not its backdrop.

The Dutch researcher Pek van Andel assembled the largest known collection of serendipitous discoveries in science and systematized them by pattern[^8]. His conclusion is analogous: the "unsought finding" has stable configurations, and some of them are programmable—the conditions for them can be deliberately created. Steven Johnson, in *Where Good Ideas Come From*—a popular book, but one grounded in historical data—separately notes that the most important discoveries in large laboratories were born not alone at the test tubes but at regular working meetings where researchers informally discussed what they were up to[^9].

The three lines—incubation, weak ties, serendipity—describe different aspects of one and the same process. The cognitive one: what happens in my head. The network one: why particular interlocutors work and others do not. The serendipitous one: what social configuration makes such meetings possible. Together they cover the phenomenon fully enough to stop calling it mystical.

## 3. Three Conditions for the Effect <a id="3-conditions"></a>

Out of everything said so far, there is one thing I want to underscore separately. This effect does not arise from just any contact with smart people. Many very smart people do not produce it at all. It is not tied to an interlocutor's formal qualifications, status, or reputation.

It usually arises when three conditions coincide at once:

1. **The person thinks differently than I do.** If my interlocutor's map of associations resembles mine, no rearrangement takes place. We circulate through the same nodes, exchanging information both of us roughly already know. What is needed is cognitive distance. Not a distance of competence but a distance of mode: a different discipline, a different school, different experience, different instruments of thought. This is exactly what sociology describes through structural holes, and what Scott Page, in his work on cognitive diversity, demonstrates with mathematical models: a group in which each member searches a different part of the solution space systematically outperforms a group of equally capable people searching the same region[^10].

2. **The level of competence is sufficiently high.** Otherwise the conversation slides into explaining basics: one side spends its energy lifting the other up to its level, and no energy is left for exploration. This is not about snobbery. It is about the fact that conversation as an instrument works at a certain altitude—when both interlocutors can let go of explanatory mode and take up something more interesting. Aral and Van Alstyne offer a refinement in the same direction: a weak tie is valuable when the partner has depth of knowledge, not only diversity.

3. **The conversation is safe and exploratory, not competitive.** In my experience this is the most important condition—and at the same time the rarest. When people argue, a different mode is at work: each defends their own position, hunts for vulnerabilities in the other's, holds onto face. In that mode new ideas do not arise, because any new thought is a concession, a weakening of one's position. When, instead of argument, a joint exploration of the unknown begins—"let's figure out together what is going on here"—the probability of delayed insights rises sharply. For both interlocutors.

Empirically close confirmation came from Woolley and colleagues in *Science* (2010): they discovered the existence of the c-factor—a general factor of a group's collective intelligence—and showed that it correlates almost not at all with the participants' average or maximum IQ[^11]. What it does correlate with: the average social sensitivity of the group members and the evenness of the distribution of speaking turns. Collective intelligence is neither the sum of individual minds nor their maximum. It is a **function of precisely how people talk to one another**.

The three conditions are rare and poorly predictable from formal markers. You can meet a renowned expert and get nothing, because the conversation turns out to be competitive or because the two of you think alike. You can meet a young person from an entirely different field and get the maximum. I stopped orienting by an interlocutor's status long ago. I orient by the trace that remains several days later.

## 4. Why This Matters Now <a id="4-now"></a>

In ordinary times this would be a quiet observational subject. Now it has become sharper, for three reasons.

The first is structural. Most professional communication today has turned into performance. Signaling at conferences, networking aimed at the useful contact, sales conversations in marketing packaging, Zoom presentations with the conclusion known in advance. I am not saying this is bad: it has its functions. I am saying that **it is structurally incompatible with what this essay is about**. In performance, both participants are defending a position or selling one. The third condition—a safe exploratory field—is absent by definition, because in performance any sincere uncertainty reads as weakness.

As a result, deep conversations have become a scarce resource. Their weight in the total time spent on professional communication has fallen and continues to fall. If twenty years ago at a conference you could count on a dozen chance conversations, at least one of which would land—today the same format is filled with structured networking, contact speed-dating, and optimized formats in which there is simply no physical pause for the delayed effect.

Second: remote work. I am not an opponent of remote: it has many advantages. But one effect it has removed almost entirely: chance encounters in the hallway, at the coffee machine, in the smoking area, on the way from the subway to the office. That was the infrastructure of serendipity in Merton's sense—a social structure that brought together people from different teams and clusters without a special occasion. On Zoom, that layer does not exist in principle. All meetings are now planned: every participant knows in advance why they came. Serendipity does not fit into that.

The third force—a partially compensating one—is conversational AI. The story here is nontrivial. A large language model formally satisfies at least two of the three conditions. It **thinks differently**—its map of associations differs radically from mine in structure; it has passed through a corpus I never passed through. It has **high competence** across a broad class of questions. And, importantly, a conversation with it is **noncompetitive by default**—the model does not defend a position, does not fight for status, does not try to win an argument.

The empirical picture is consistent: in a large field study, Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2023) found that the productivity of support agents at a large company rose by 14% on average after the introduction of generative AI—by 34% for novices, and nearly zero for experienced professionals[^12]. The authors' interpretation: AI propagates the patterns of more capable workers and shortens the path to competence for those who do not yet have an internal "school" of strong interlocutors. This describes the honest zone of AI's work as a cognitive partner—it covers the "differently thinking interlocutor" condition for those who simply do not have one in their life.

What AI does not provide is **physical presence and the slow background continuation** that works with living people. A live conversation carries the load of body, emotion, voice—everything that, in neurobiological terms, engages a different memory system and fixes the conversation as an event rather than a set of utterances. Moreover, after meeting a person I physically move through space—I walk, I ride transit—and that motor pause coincides with what Sio and Ormerod describe as a cognitively light filling of the incubation period. After a session with an AI, I usually just close the tab and switch to the next task—there is no pause, and there is not much to return to.

For me, this thread closes with a single sentence. **The most useful conversations are not the ones after which you have an answer, but the ones after which you have new questions.** Answers give closure. Questions give trajectory. The effect I am talking about is about trajectory, not closure.

## 5. What to Do About It <a id="5-what-to-do"></a>

There is one important thing you cannot do with this effect: you cannot organize it. It does not fit into a schedule, does not reproduce by protocol, does not scale through structured networking. When I tried—and I did try—the result was empty. Any attempt to hold a "useful conversation by plan" kills the third condition, exploratory safety, and the effect vanishes.

What you can do is create the **conditions**, not the effect itself. Choose conversations with people who think differently. Enter them in the mode of an explorer rather than an expert, even if on the topic you are an expert. Do not try to assess a conversation's usefulness in the first hours—there is almost always nothing there, and that is normal.

The main methodological consequence I have drawn for myself: **the quality of a conversation is assessed several days later, not right away**. If after three or four days nothing has appeared, the effect was not present in this case, and the conversation goes into a different category (informational, social, whatever—but not the one this essay is about). If something has shifted, the meeting was real, and the interlocutor is worth remembering as a long-horizon resource.

I am deliberately not ending with "five rules for effective conversations with smart people." If such rules worked, the effect would have ceased to exist long ago—it rests precisely on not being reducible to procedure. This is one of those cases where observation is more useful than instruction. I prefer to leave the reader with the phenomenon itself and its three conditions rather than with a checklist that turns conversation back into performance.

The only thing I would ask you to keep in mind after this text is not to confuse the genres of conversation. Most professional communication has no relation to the effect described here, and it should not. These are different instruments for different tasks. But if, every so often, a meeting appears in the schedule where the three conditions coincide—it is worth protecting, and worth not evaluating right away. The effect will come in a few days, or it will never come. That is not a bug—that is the phenomenon itself.
