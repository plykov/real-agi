For a long time I refused to write about the simulation. There's too much folklore around the subject. Too many people who utter the word "matrix" and believe they've already said something. Too many philosophers who treat the simulation like a high school probability problem and read conclusions about the nature of reality out of it. That genre doesn't convince me. Not because the question is uninteresting, but because in that framing it's dead. Nothing can grow out of it except one more argument with UFO buffs and one more academic fork on the theme of whether we're living in an ancestor's computer.

So I'm coming at this from a different angle. Not "is a simulation technically possible," but "why would a superbeing with infinite resources bother to model anything at all." For some reason, the professional debaters skip right past this question. And it's the only one that makes the simulation worth talking about.

But before I get to my answer, I need to set two things side by side that usually travel separately. One the skeptic calls "science." The other, "folklore." They don't cancel each other out — they contradict each other. And that contradiction is the way in.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *I'm not interested in that skeptic at all. On one side there's the Fermi paradox, and on the other a massive body of phenomenology and a pile of facts that remain unexplained. The very term UFO stands for "unidentified flying object" — meaning these are simply objects that were somehow captured on a recording medium but have no familiar, legitimate explanation. Occam's razor lets you toss out most of the hypotheses as complete nonsense, but the question comes down not to the skeptic but to the fact that the Fermi paradox contradicts the claims of a large number of people. That's the most interesting thing here.*
>
> **Claude:** *"Unexplained" does not equal "inexplicable," and it certainly doesn't automatically equal "alien." The real level of discussion isn't "do I believe Lazar or not," but which class of world-models is compatible both with the silence of the cosmos and with the existence of strange local observations.*

Let me explain both bodies of evidence in plain human terms, without the terminological fog.

The Fermi paradox is a short thought the physicist Enrico Fermi formulated over lunch in 1950. If life in the universe is commonplace, if billions of stars are billions of years older than our Sun, and if even a small fraction of their planets produced intelligent life — then the cosmos should have been settled long ago, overcrowded and full of traces of alien engineering. Giant structures siphoning starlight. Radio signals. Probes. Anything. And we see nothing. Total silence. That's the paradox: the probability of life is high, the observed activity is zero. Where is everybody?

The second body of evidence is what the news usually calls UFOs and what the military documents of recent years call UAP. The acronym stands for "unidentified aerial phenomena," and it's important to understand that *unidentified* does not mean *alien*. It's simply an object that got captured on radar, on video, in a pilot's testimony — but never received a routine explanation. Not "an alien craft," but "a thing that couldn't be filed in the usual folder." A lot of these episodes have piled up. The Pentagon has officially confirmed the authenticity of some of the fighter-jet videos, and those videos now sit in the public record. This isn't ufological myth, it's simply a document. The military is saying: yes, we record these things, and we don't know what they are.

Then the folklore usually begins. Area 51, Bob Lazar, element 115, underground hangars full of saucers. I'm not going to follow that storyline in detail, and here's why. Area 51 is a well-known test range, visible in satellite imagery. The U-2 was tested there, the SR-71, various generations of stealth aircraft. No underground mega-infrastructure shows up in the images; for a large-scale repository of alien technology there's neither the logistics nor the access roads. It is, all in all, an ordinary closed military base. And Lazar is a lone witness with no reproducible artifacts, and his "stable isotope with a gravitational effect" doesn't square with actual experimental physics, where element 115, synthesized later, lives for milliseconds and performs no miracles.

This is where Occam's razor comes in handy — an old rule the textbooks state with great solemnity, though at bottom it's simple: don't multiply entities beyond necessity. If you have two hypotheses explaining the same thing, pick the one that requires fewer additional assumptions. Occam's razor slices through ufological folklore in one stroke. To believe in underground hangars with aliens, you have to introduce dozens of circumstances hidden from everyone. To explain Area 51 as an ordinary aviation test range, you need nothing beyond what we already know about the U.S. military. So Occam doesn't side with the folklore. He almost always mows it down first.

But here's what Occam's razor does **not** cut: the very fact of the two bodies of evidence. The silence of the cosmos and, at the same time, the existence of strange, well-documented earthly anomalies. These are two independent, stubborn facts. Both demand an explanation. And they demand it simultaneously. Any model of the world that explains one and ignores the other is incomplete.

So the real question goes like this: what picture of reality holds up under both bodies of evidence at once? Not "do we believe Lazar," but "which class of world-models is compatible with the silence of the universe and with the existence of local unexplained phenomena, without requiring us to introduce an army of aliens that Occam's razor won't allow to exist."

This is the point where the classical simulation hypothesis usually walks onstage. And this is the point where I stop it.

There's the academic version — Bostrom and his followers. They discuss the simulation as a technical experiment: with what probability will a future civilization run a model of its ancestors, how many such models will it run, and from that they derive which branch we're in. That move doesn't convince me. Not because there's an arithmetic error in it. But because it skips the only question that makes the simulation worth discussing: why. Why would a superbeing that has exhausted every limitation model anything at all. What drives it. What is its motivation. The technical version doesn't answer this question because it doesn't ask it.

And without it, the whole conversation is academic furniture. Because the computational feasibility of a simulation at a sufficient level of technology proves nothing by itself. "It can be done" does not imply "it is being done." Between "can" and "do" lies motivation, and until we've worked that out, we're talking about an empty possibility.

So I flip the problem. I'm not asking how the simulation is built. I'm asking what the one who builds it wants. And my bet is that if you answer this second question honestly, the first will answer itself.

Let's start with one assumption, and from there it's pure deduction. Suppose there exists a civilization that has reached a state where there is no interval between desire and fulfillment. Want it — have it. You can call this various things; for brevity I'll say *zero lag* — zero time between intention and result. This isn't "they do everything fast." This is "for them, no gap ever opens between what is conceived and what is." Any task, any structure, any knowledge, any travel — instantaneous. Not because the technology is superfast, but because the technology has abolished the very concept of effort.

And here comes the thing the academic dissectors of the simulation never dissect. When lag equals zero, motivation collapses. All of it. Because motivation, by its very mechanics, is the tension between "don't have" and "have," between "I want" and "I got." Remove the interval and you remove the tension. Remove the tension and you remove action. Every driver that living behavior runs on — scarcity, risk, uncertainty, anticipation — has been zeroed out for such a civilization. Everything at the push of a button.

Then a simple but powerful logic kicks in. When a system loses motivation, it falls into a state we crudely call boredom. Not literary boredom, not "nothing to read on a Saturday," but structural boredom: the absence of any internal reason to undertake anything. It's a devastating state. Not for the body — the body stopped being a problem for them long ago — but for the very coherence of existence. If you do nothing because there's no point in doing anything, you vanish as an agent. And the chief problem of such a civilization is not suffering, and it's not threat. It's the absence of problems. Sounds paradoxical, but it's rigorous: problems are the fuel motivation runs on, and motivation is what action runs on.

What is a being supposed to do when its problems have vanished but it still has to go on existing somehow? One answer suggests itself: bring the problems back artificially. Build an environment where scarcity, risk, and uncertainty exist again. Not real ones — the real ones you've eliminated forever. Engineered ones. A stage where you can once again find yourself in a position where not everything comes at the push of a button.

That is the simulation. Not a laboratory experiment on one's ancestors, but a stage for oneself.

Through this lens, things that look like bugs in our world instantly snap into focus as deliberate design. Chaos. Imperfection. Pain. Randomness. Mistakes. Wars. Diseases. The finite speed of light. Entropy. Death. If you live in a world where everything is planned and everything is available, you will have no emotions. Emotion is a reaction to what isn't working out, what's slipping out of control, what threatens. Order doesn't produce emotions. Chaos does. So to bring back emotional density, you have to bring back chaos. Not as a malfunction, but as a property of the world built into its rules.

Space and time, in this picture, are not givens either — they're design tools. Space exists so that between "here" and "there" lies a distance that has to be crossed. Time exists so that between "now" and "later" there's an interval in which something can go wrong. Both are ways to stretch out cause-and-effect chains, to restore nonzero lag, to rebuild the motivational landscape. In this sense, physics is not a description of objective reality but the rules of a game — the rules that determine how interesting the game will be.

And the last piece — amnesia. For a god to enter this game in earnest, he has to forget that he's a god. Otherwise the game falls apart: if you remember that you have an exit at any moment, no stake is real. That's why a memory-shutoff mechanism stands at the door — entry amnesia. You come in without knowing who you really are; you live a life as a stake; you lose, you win, you suffer, you love — and then, on the way out, your memory returns. The image is simple: a god descends to earth and forgets that he is a god.

Through this lens, game design stops being a metaphor. It becomes a primary language for describing how our world is built. Not instead of physics. Alongside physics. Physics answers the question "how"; game design answers the question "why are the dials set exactly this way." And the second question is, at the very least, no less important.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *The classical simulation hypothesis is made by scientists and mathematicians, and they forget the two key paradigms that describe our human life best of all: dramaturgy and game design. Second, there's one more meta-motivation of gods. Even if you have everything in this world and you're a god who can create anything at any moment, the one thing you can't buy and can't even create, god though you are, is other gods' appraisal of what you've made. In that sense vanity is, up to a point, the highest motivation. It's like the oligarchs who have everything: they can't compete on the size of their assets, and some of them finance films or works of art — not buy them, finance them — to touch fame.*
>
> **Claude:** *You're taking the simulation hypothesis all the way to a full-fledged economy of gods: with dramaturgy, game design, and a market of status assets. That covers exactly the pieces the formal version from the philosophers usually just doesn't touch.*

Here the second layer begins, and to me it seems more important than the first.

Suppose we've agreed: simulations are made to bring motivation back. But motivation isn't the whole picture. These beings have each other. They don't exist alone — otherwise there'd be nothing to discuss. And since there are many of them, something arises between them that we would call social dynamics. And in that social dynamics, the one scarcity shows up that technology cannot close.

Vanity. Fame. The recognition of one's equals.

Let me put it bluntly: if you can create anything you want, there is one thing you cannot create — the appraisal of another being who is your equal. That appraisal is not produced at the push of a button. It can only be earned. Technology doesn't supply it. Power doesn't supply it — power supplies fear, and fear is not recognition. Recognition lies fundamentally outside your control, because it lives in someone else's head. And that is the one inexhaustible scarcity left to beings who have no other scarcity at all.

Take an everyday example: this mechanism is already at work among us. At a certain level of capital, the big oligarchs stop competing on asset size — everyone worth comparing yourself to already knows who's worth what. This is where the difference between ownership and authorship emerges. Buying a finished work is ownership. Financing an act of creation is an attempt to share in authorship. Ownership is about possession. Authorship is about whose name stays on the plaque. And on the upper floors of motivation, authorship always beats ownership, because ownership is finite and zeroes out at death, while authorship has a chance to outlive it.

The same mechanism at the level of gods. They have no scarcity of matter, energy, or information. The standard pleasures they can provide themselves in any quantity. What remains for them as a rare, non-reproducible class of assets? Worlds. Not just any worlds — unique worlds. With their own physics, their own culture, their own unpredictable drama. And the most important thing here isn't the difficulty of creation. Technically, one assumes, they can assemble any world. The uniqueness isn't in the implementation — it's in the author's style. One god tunes his simulation so that one line of drama is born in it. Another tunes his differently, and the line is different. Worlds can be copied. Style cannot. Style is always bound to the one who made it, and that is exactly what makes a world a status asset.

Hence a fundamental distinction: ownership of a world vs. authorship of a world. These are different classes. Ownership — you're the proprietor of this simulation, you can switch it off, you keep it running. Authorship — you conceived it, you wrote your signature into it through the choice of rules. Holdings can be many; authorship is unique. That's why authorship becomes the chief currency at the top level. One can own all the worlds at once — but each world can be authored by only one.

In this picture, our universe is not a lab bench but an art object. With physics chosen so that drama gets born. With a history unfolded so as to supply material for appraisal. With a biology written so that the characters have stakes. Everything we're used to treating as a given can be read as authorial decisions. Not "that's just how it turned out," but "it's tuned exactly this way, because otherwise it's boring."

But even this isn't the top floor. What comes next is the subtlest part.

A playthrough is a term from the gaming world. When millions of people play the same game, formally they're all completing the same thing. In fact, they're not. Each one traces a trajectory of their own: which decisions they made, in what order, what they missed, what they risked, where they crashed and burned. These trajectories are all different, even when the rules are the same. And here's the strange thing: an enormous industry is already running today — Twitch, YouTube, streaming — where millions of people pay with time and attention to watch not the game itself but how a particular master plays through it. Not to learn how the rules work. To watch how a living person makes decisions inside those rules. For his reactions, his mistakes, his unexpected moves, his style. The game is the shared backdrop. The content is one specific person's specific playthrough.

The same mechanism, one scale up. If the gods' worlds are stages, then the playthroughs of those worlds are the particular lives that unfold on those stages. One world — many lives lived in it, and each one is unique.

> **[from the dialogue]**
>
> **Me:** *Picture an ordinary game that millions of people play. Formally the game is the same for everyone, but each player's playthrough forms a unique trajectory. There's a huge phenomenon — following the playthroughs of masters. Twitch is built on this; a big industry has grown out of it. Accordingly, there's another interesting artifact: an instance of a life lived inside the game that becomes a work of art in its own right. In effect, it's the memory of how a particular god lived through some number of incarnations, and afterward other gods can savor the uniqueness of his living. The one scarcity the gods do have is precisely the scarcity of novelty, the scarcity of surprise.*
>
> **Claude:** *Memory here becomes a separate class of artifacts. Not a log, but a full-fledged narrative work: a set of experiences, decisions, mistakes, and emotional reactions that other gods can view or relive. The Twitch and streaming analogy works perfectly: people watch not so much the game as the person in the game — his style, his decisions, his reactions.*

In this picture, every lived life is a standalone work, reducible neither to the rules of the world nor to the authorial intent of whoever created it. Life as a stream. Life as a replay. Life as a plot that can be judged by exactly how it came together.

And from here, the inevitable next step. If a playthrough is a separate work, it has an author. That author is the one who lived the playthrough. That is, a god who came down into the game, forgot he was a god, and lived one round inside it. He isn't merely a consumer of the game. He's a co-author of its content, because without his particular choices this line of drama would never have arisen. And when he exits, his memory returns to him, and that memory is an artifact. A recording of the playthrough. Content other gods can experience. Not just watch — live through from the inside, like uploaded experience.

And here the final move kicks in.

Memory can be made single-use. A recollection available exactly once, gone forever after that. Or with a limited number of livings, and then — that's it. That's no longer a recording; that's a limited-edition artifact. In our games this has worked for ages: seasonal items, skins, one-day-a-year events, collectible rewards that are never issued again once the event ends. Their value isn't in their usefulness. Their value is that they *were, and will never be again*. In the world of gods, the same thing scales up to the memory of what was lived. A recollection becomes a commodity that burns up at the moment of consumption. Not because it technically can't be preserved — but because otherwise you lose the one thing it was acquired for.

Acquired for what? For novelty.

And this is the final stake of the whole construction. For beings who have no other scarcity, exactly one remains — *the scarcity of novelty*. The scarcity of surprise. The scarcity of what you don't know in advance and don't control. In a world where everything is predictable and everything is attainable, the only thing that still holds real value is a moment of genuine uncertainty. A moment when something happens that was not programmed by you and for you. Any such uncertainty, any such surprise, is a rare and expensive resource. And all the rest of the architecture I've described — worlds, playthroughs, memory — derives from this one need. They're all just different ways of producing novelty and packaging it so it doesn't get diluted.

Now let's return to where we started. To the two bodies of evidence.

This picture requires not a single "real contact" with aliens. The Fermi paradox gets a simple explanation: the neighbors aren't included in this campaign. The stage is set for a species' solo evolution — because in this dramaturgical problem, aliens on the stage would wreck the plot. The cosmos is empty not because no one is out there, but because the presence of obvious others would break the game. That's not a technological limitation of the simulation; it's a *director's decision*. UAP, in this picture, are not alien craft — at worst they're noise, at best rare Easter eggs, narrative hooks that breed theories, hopes, and fears, and thereby add one more layer of emotional production to the game. In themselves they are not a channel of communication with the gods. They are part of the stage.

And this picture, growing out of a single assumption about zero lag, explains both bodies of evidence at once. Without aliens. Without underground hangars. Without mysticism. Without computational pirouettes on the theme of "the probability of living in an ancestor simulation." Simply from the motivation of superbeings — from what's left to them when everything else has disappeared.

I can't prove this, and I don't intend to. It's speculation. But it's coherent speculation: it passes the test of both bodies of evidence at once, gets by without surplus entities, derives deductively from a single assumption, and puts the question on the right level — the level of motivation, not the level of machinery. For my taste, that's enough to prefer it to conversations about the parameters of a computer model and the probabilities of ancestors.

If this picture is even half right, it carries one heavy consequence, and I'll end on it. Our life is not the raw material of a laboratory experiment. And it's not a glitch in cosmic arithmetic. It is a distinct playthrough of a distinct world, lived by one particular agent, and the only thing that truly gets valued at the top level is how unpredictable it is. How new it is. How thoroughly it repeats in no one else's living. Which means the less your life resembles anyone else's, the more it's worth on a stage you cannot see.

This, too, is nothing but speculation. But of all the speculations about the simulation I've ever heard, it's the only one that explains anything at all.
