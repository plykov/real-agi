# Cartographers of the Keyhole

*In dreams, nothing is ever absurd. Follow that observation all the way down, and the entire system of knowledge comes into question—along with the habit of treating wakefulness as the baseline state of consciousness.*

## TL;DR <a id="tldr"></a>

In dreams, everything makes sense—not because dreams are full of hidden meaning, but because the mechanism that registers cognitive rupture in waking life is switched off. Absurdity is the observer's reaction, not a property of the event: awake, meaning is born from checking experience against a model of the world; in dreams, from the continuity of experience. From there the chain unwinds. Knowledge as we practice it—naming things, establishing connections, formalizing, testing for falsifiability—turns out to be a form of control, and control grows out of fear. We have no direct access to the dream: we study not the dream but the dream report. That is how a third of human life ended up outside science and philosophy, both built as if only wakefulness existed—while wakefulness itself was appointed the baseline state on no grounds whatsoever. We are not expanding knowledge—we are mapping the keyhole's opening with ever-greater precision, having forgotten that the door exists. It does not follow that everything beyond the door is radically different. What follows is this: the scale of our confidence far exceeds the scale of our grounds.

## 1. In Dreams, Nothing Is Ever Absurd <a id="no-absurdity"></a>

In dreams, everything makes sense. Awake, we constantly draw a line: this over here is meaningful, rational, even if mythological—and that over there is nonsense. We call something meaningless when we subjectively cannot find coherent consistency in it; when it triggers no familiar narrative; when it doesn't resolve into anything even remotely recognizable; when we perceive it but cannot identify it or connect it to anything we know, so that it provokes no reaction at all—not even an emotional one. Dreams have no such problem. Inside a dream, everything is orderly, harmonious, consistent: the logic of events, phenomena, and objects raises no questions. In dreams, nothing is ever absurd.

The objection writes itself: dreams are nothing but absurdity. A dead relative turns out to be your old schoolteacher at the same time, a house is a train, and the train is your childhood; the dreamer talks to the house and isn't surprised when it answers. From the standpoint of waking consciousness—gross violations of logic, identity, and causality. But the objection swaps out the main thing. Absurdity is not an objective property of an event. Absurdity is an evaluative category, a reaction, the experience of cognitive rupture: "wait, something doesn't add up here." That reaction is what dreams lack. The dreamer lives through scenes that are absurd to the daytime eye without experiencing them as absurd. What disappears in the dream is not contradiction—it is the mechanism that registers contradiction. The difference is fundamental: the first would be a property of the dream world; the second is a property of the one watching.

The difference between the two modes fits into a short formula. Awake, meaning arises through correspondence to stable models of the world: we continuously check incoming experience against what we already know. Cognitive science describes this as the work of the predictive brain—it constantly compares the stream of perception against its model of the world and sounds the alarm at a prediction error[^predictive]. When no compatibility is found, a whole spectrum of reactions fires: ridiculous, strange, impossible, absurd, "that can't happen." In dreams, meaning arises differently—through the continuity of experience. The dream doesn't care that an object contradicts itself. Only one thing matters: the next moment flows emotionally and associatively out of the previous one. What operates is not the logic of causes but the logic of transitions. Not "how is this possible?" but "yes, this is exactly how it should be." This is not an absence of meaning. It is a different type of meaning—and it works without fail.

Dreaming consciousness doesn't ask "how is this connected?"—it proceeds from the assumption that it already is. Any new element instantly slots into the current picture; the dream world has an almost infinite capacity to assimilate anomalies. Whatever appears immediately becomes part of the context: awake, such a takeover would be stopped cold by verification; in the dream, there is no one to stop it. The web of connections that daytime experience enters only with effort and inspection becomes incredibly plastic in dreams—it instantly joins anything to anything, and almost any image turns out to be meaningful. The filter that checks waking experience for compatibility with the model of the world is switched off in dreams, or radically weakened—and physiology doesn't object here; it plays along: in REM sleep, a region of the prefrontal cortex is selectively dampened—and with it, logical control and reflective self-observation are suppressed, so the circuit that stands watch for contradictions by day is simply taken off duty at night[^prefrontal].

The perception of young children is built in a similar way—child psychology long ago described how a child does not infer connections between things but proceeds from their existence[^piaget]. Mythological thinking works the same way; the ethnology of the early twentieth century called it *participation mystique*: the connection is not established—it is presumed to already exist, even if it's not yet clear what kind of connection it is[^mythic].

Hence a conclusion that looks modest and weighs a great deal. Meaninglessness is the state of an observer who cannot fit what he perceives into the web of connections available to him. Not a property of the world—a property of the encounter with the world. Not "there is nonsense in the world, and we detect it," but "there is a boundary in the observer, and he calls nonsense everything that fails to pass through it." And then perhaps we don't divide the world into the meaningful and the meaningless at all. Perhaps we continuously produce meaning, and "meaningless" is what we call the things for which the meaning-production machinery has temporarily failed to find a suitable template.

## 2. Knowledge as Control, Control as Fear <a id="control-fear"></a>

If the mechanism that registers contradiction can be switched off—and the dream demonstrates every night that it can—the question turns to the mechanism itself. What is it doing while we're awake? Whom does it serve?

Dreams are interesting not because they communicate some truths about the world—that would be dream-dictionary-level naivety. Dreams are interesting because they demonstrate a different mode of consciousness at work. We are used to thinking that knowledge is possible only through more control, more analysis, more classification, more verification. The dream shows a state in which a large share of those mechanisms is shut down while consciousness keeps generating a coherent world. If dreams expand anything, it is not the list of answers but the space of what can be perceived at all: they don't answer questions—they change the very structure of the question. Waking consciousness studies the world through its filters and almost never studies the filters themselves. Dreaming is one of the few states where consciousness can be caught in a different configuration—where its workings can be seen from an unfamiliar angle.

People have moved in this direction for a long time, and seriously. Tibetan dream and sleep yoga builds its practice around maintaining awareness through dreaming and deep sleep[^dreamyoga]. Researchers of lucid dreaming have shown experimentally that awareness inside a dream is a recordable phenomenon: sleepers sent pre-arranged eye-movement signals straight from REM sleep[^lucid]. Precision matters here: this is a fact about the existence of traditions and research programs—not a recipe, not proof of secret knowledge, and not an invitation to practice.

And yet the very program of "learning to stay aware in a dream without destroying the dream's logic" contains a fatal error. It is an attempt to stuff our habitual awareness into the dream—a clinging to it, a fear of losing control that has reached even the study of dreams. We want to enter a different mode of consciousness without leaving our own: to carry into the dream a rational subject who will maintain continuity, remember everything, describe it, and verify it. The same trouble plagues the stock explanation that "dreams are the internal processing of experience." We do not know what dreaming is at all. There are several hypotheses; "processing of experience" is just one of them, and it is so toothless, so safe, that no one wants to refute it. The radical alternative sounds less comfortable: dreams are interesting not as an object of study—dreams are interesting as a state in which the very need to study disappears. For rational consciousness this is nearly impossible to accept. The very commitment to awareness and control may itself be part of the filter: we are so used to counting as knowledge only what can be verbalized, formalized, and reproduced that we exclude vast regions of experience in advance—not having tested them, simply having declined to recognize them as experience.

It's worth spelling out what we even call knowledge. To know is to name, to establish connections, to formalize, to describe, to falsify—to frame a claim so it can be tested for refutation. Each of these operations is legitimate on its own. But the full set serves one purpose: after running it, we feel in control of reality. The list looks like a methodology and works like a sedative. And beneath the feeling of control lies fear.

Rational consciousness lives through the distance between subject and object. To know something, you must step back, single it out, name it, compare it—the very grammar of knowing requires two parties: the one who looks and the thing being looked at. The dream, by contrast, looks like participation in something that has not yet split into subject and object—and that is where the anxiety comes from. Nearly all of Western philosophy is built around control, distinction, analysis, proof, and this is not merely an intellectual preference. It is an existential need to hold a stable world in place. We grant reality only to what we can establish cognitive sovereignty over—the rule of our own categories. Can't classify it—we call it chaos. Can't explain it—we call it nonsense. Can't control it—we call it illusion. Can't predict it—we call it chance. Four words we use to mask four defeats.

Then the drive for meaning turns out to be not a search for truth but a defense mechanism. Not "I want to understand the world." But "I want the world to stay understandable enough that I never have to face its immensity."

## 3. Interlude: How Language Slips the Universe a Face <a id="teleology"></a>

Here the argument nearly drove into a ditch, and honesty requires showing exactly how. At the phrase "the drive for meaning is a defense mechanism," a beautiful addition begs to be written: but fear is only one side; the other is that the Universe itself is building local islands of order through us. Sounds deep. I catch myself with the sentence almost on the page—and inside it, contraband: the Universe has suddenly acquired agency, intention, and purpose, and we have become the instrument of its design. Not one of those assumptions follows from anywhere. In the middle of a text about meaning being a property of the observer, I nearly ascribed subjecthood to the Universe. Philosophy damns people for that—and rightly so. The funniest part is that the trap sprang inside an argument written precisely about such traps: language is stronger than the writer's intentions and drags teleology through every crack the moment talk turns to the whole.

It is an old trap, the teleological one: ascribing purpose where none has been shown. As soon as the subject is the whole, language starts humanizing it automatically. The verbs "strives," "tries," "creates," "chooses" quietly smuggle in a subject. Metaphysical contraband with a long pedigree: the habit of endowing the cosmos with a face, a character, and a development plan runs through the entire history of philosophy[^teleology]—and the evidence for it is exactly equal to the evidence against.

The correct formulation is duller and more honest. In some region of reality, structures arise that we call consciousness. These structures produce meaning, order, models, and explanations. It does not follow that reality as a whole has anything whatsoever to do with meaning, order, or explanations.

Then the argument turns on its own categories—and things get uncomfortable. The reasoning began with meaning being a property of the observer, not of the Universe. But the same move strikes "order" and "chaos," "logic," "causality," "objects," "time." It may be that we know not a single property of the Universe—we know only the properties of our way of interacting with it. Kant distinguished the phenomenon—the world as it is given to us—from the noumenon, the "thing-in-itself" that is inaccessible to us[^kant]. Here the same thought is pushed to the limit—the Kantian catastrophe at full scale: what's in question is not a portion of knowledge but its entire vocabulary.

## 4. We Study Not the Dream but the Dream Report <a id="dream-report"></a>

It helps to separate two levels. On sleep as a physiological process, the data exist and are decent: stages, eye movements, brain activity, hormones, the effects of sleep deprivation; the anchor point of that science—the discovery of REM sleep—dates to 1953[^rem]. No complaint there. The complaint concerns the data on the content of the experience: stages and activity are measured by instruments; the content of the experience is not—its place in the data is taken by a story. The entire world of the dream is available to us almost exclusively through a report after waking. The scheme runs like this: there was some experience; then waking; then waking consciousness tries to recall the experience; then translates it into language; then tells another person. And only after the fifth step does the thing appear that a researcher calls "dream data"—in the literature it is honestly named the dream report[^dreamreports]. Five steps—and at each one the original experience is reprocessed, by a system that is already running on different rules by the time it does the processing.

Looked at strictly, the situation is wild. We do not study the dream. We study the story about the dream—composed by a consciousness already operating in a different mode. Roughly like studying the ocean from the memories of a man who briefly surfaced. The word "data" barely holds here at all: between the object and the data stands not an instrument but an awakening—a transition out of the very state of consciousness we are trying to judge. Something is lost at every link of the chain: recall retains crumbs; translation into language straightens out what never lived in language to begin with. Hence the characteristic property of nearly all reports: the person is certain the dream contained an enormous amount of content, yet by morning a few fragments remain, along with the feeling of having lost something far larger. That certainty itself is corroborated by nothing—it, too, is part of the story.

Science demands observability, reproducibility, and independent verification. Dreaming—one of the most regular experiences of human existence—escapes all three. A dream cannot be observed from the outside; it cannot be reproduced on demand; the only thing that can be independently verified is the story, and the story is already the product of another state. And this is not because dreams don't exist. It is because the act of observation itself changes the mode of the consciousness we are trying to investigate: to record a dream, you have to wake up, and the one who wakes is no longer the one who dreamed. It resembles a limiting case of the observer effect—the term physics uses for a measurement that disturbs the system being measured[^observer]. An analogy, not an identity: there is no quantum mechanics in dreams; there is the same structural snare—the instrument of access destroys the thing it gives access to.

So the precise formula is not "we lack data on dreams." The precise formula is: we have almost no direct access to the object of study. That claim is stronger. A shortage of data is cured by time, money, and instruments. The absence of direct access is cured by none of the above—no instrument will undo the fact that between a dream and its researcher there always stands an awakening: the problem of dreams turns out to be not a problem of missing information but a problem of the way of knowing itself. Dreams are inconvenient not because they are poorly studied. They are inconvenient because they expose the limits of the method we are accustomed to using on reality. Not the limits of the dream—the limits of the observer.

## 5. A Third of Life, Written Off as Noise <a id="one-third"></a>

The scale of the problem gets understated at the level of wording itself: sleep is politely called "one of the phenomena of human existence." Sleep is not one of the phenomena. Sleep is roughly a third of an entire human life: the consensus in sleep medicine recommends seven or more hours of sleep a night for an adult, and simple arithmetic turns those hours into a third of every day—every single day, for life[^sleepthird]. Not a third of waking hours. Not a third of mental activity. A third of existence. And that third is ignored completely—if you don't count fortune-tellers, dream dictionaries, and the rest of the mystification, which I am bracketing out immediately and entirely: it has nothing to do with a conversation about knowledge.

It is hard to imagine another region of experience on this scale about which one could calmly say: "well, yes, there are some hypotheses, but on the whole it's not very important." Seven to eight hours out of twenty-four, every day, for every human being—decades, when tallied over a lifetime; and the status of an optional footnote to real life. Anywhere else, the phrase "there are some hypotheses" would sound like the opening of a major research program. Applied to sleep, it sounds like the end of the conversation.

The reason is not only the difficulty of the research, though the previous section showed how great that is. The reason runs deeper: sleep does not fit the basic cultural metaphor of modernity. Modern civilization is built around action—work, decisions, production, communication, control, measurement. In that coordinate system, sleep produces nothing observable. It creates no artifacts. Leaves no records. Generates no verifiable results. Fits into no economy. A person awake does things, and what's done can be counted; a person asleep passes through that ledger as equipment downtime. So sleep ends up at the periphery of attention—even though, by volume of experience, it rivals the whole of waking life.

Put harshly: humanity has two enormous domains of existence—waking and sleep—and nearly all of our philosophy, science, and epistemology (the branch of philosophy about how we come to know anything at all and why it deserves trust) are built as if only the first one existed. An exaggeration—but not as much of one as we would like.

The weight of this charge shows against the backdrop of the first section: dreams don't merely toss up strange images—they demonstrate a mode of consciousness in which the fundamental categories of waking life—meaning, meaninglessness, subject, object, logical consistency—work entirely differently or are absent altogether. We build theories of consciousness leaning on the state that occupies two-thirds of life and ignoring the state that occupies the remaining third. If a biologist studied an animal two-thirds of the time and declared a third of its behavior secondary noise, that would be considered a very bad research strategy. With sleep we do exactly that—and even if there is no "secret knowledge" in it, methodologically it looks strange. The sheer scale of sleep simply does not match the place it holds in our picture of the human being and of consciousness.

## 6. The Keyhole <a id="keyhole"></a>

It is far worse than one biologist's bad strategy. The image I use to describe our epistemic situation is the keyhole: we stand before a locked door and examine the world through the opening meant for a key. We build a theory of the Universe while still looking at it through a keyhole—and we simply ignore everything else.

Push the thought to its limit, and the problem isn't even that the keyhole is small. The problem is that we have forgotten the door exists. We have grown so used to our way of perceiving that we have stopped perceiving it as a way of perceiving. It has become, for us, reality itself. Seeing it from the outside is nearly impossible—there is nothing to compare it to. The only thing that regularly presents each of us with an alternative mode is the dream. And the dream is precisely what we declared to be noise.

Hence a telling asymmetry. Dreams, deep meditation, mystical and psychedelic experience are conventionally called "altered states of consciousness." The very grammar of the term betrays the assumption: "altered"—relative to a norm no one has ever checked. The reverse question almost never gets asked: why is waking considered the unaltered state? Compared to what? Who proved that this particular mode is the standard? The question is not a fringe one: as early as the beginning of the 1970s, science heard a proposal to stop treating ordinary waking consciousness as the privileged reference point for studying other states[^asc]. Logically, waking is one of consciousness's operating modes. Exceptionally useful for navigation, survival, and joint activity. From that usefulness, no privileged access to the fullness of reality follows.

In this picture, the dream holds a special place. Waking life works at getting an ever-better look at what's visible through the opening. The dream is one of the few states that lets you notice the existence of the wall itself, the keyhole, and the fact that our vision is bounded by it. Knowledge of a different order: not about what's in the room, but about why we see the room the way we do.

The keyhole metaphor also overturns the usual picture of progress. We like to think we are gradually expanding the domain of knowledge. It comes out otherwise: we are mapping, with ever-greater precision, what has already fallen within the opening. Those are not the same thing. The keyhole gets polished: the optics keep improving, the resolution keeps climbing, the map keeps gaining detail—and the very diligence of this work creates the sense that we are seeing more and more of the world. We are seeing more and more of the opening. You can produce an incredibly precise map of the visible fragment and still know nothing about what lies outside the field of view. The map isn't lying—within the opening it is impeccable; the substitution begins where the map of the opening is passed off as the map of the room. And the phrase "outside the field of view" is still too weak: this is not about unknown objects that instruments haven't reached yet. It is about possible forms of experience, categories, and ways of organizing reality that do not pass through our cognitive interface at all—through the set of formats in which consciousness is able to receive the world. Through that very keyhole.

So the complaint about sleep is not the complaint that "scientists don't study sleep enough." The critique built on sleep is a critique of the entire system of knowledge. That system rests on an assumption adopted without discussion: the waking state is sufficient for describing reality. The assumption is almost never debated—it is accepted as self-evident. Sleep matters for exactly this reason: every night, in everyone without exception, it presents the fact that alongside the "baseline" mode there exists another one, in which the baseline's basic categories do not work.

One caveat remains, without which everything said here would turn into mystification with the sign flipped—a secret abyss installed where secret knowledge used to be. From the fact that we look through a keyhole, it does not yet follow that everything else differs radically from what we see. That is possible, not proven. An argument about the limits of knowledge is obliged to remember the limits of its own claims. What does follow, almost certainly, is something else: **the scale of our confidence far exceeds the scale of our grounds for that confidence**. The most unpleasant thing about the keyhole metaphor is not that we know little. It is that we may be underestimating the depth of our own ignorance—by several orders of magnitude.

## Glossary <a id="glossary"></a>

Entries are ordered by the flow of the argument, not alphabetically: the glossary reads as a brief map of the essay. The author's own concepts and metaphors come first; borrowed external concepts second, each with a short neutral definition and a note on the work it does in the text.

### The Author's Concepts and Metaphors

**The mechanism that registers cognitive rupture**—the circuit that, in waking life, continuously checks incoming experience against the model of the world and raises the alarm: "wait, something doesn't add up here." What disappears in the dream is not contradiction—what disappears is the mechanism that registers it; the difference is fundamental, because the first would be a property of the dream world, while the second is a property of the one watching.

**Meaning as a property of the observer**—the essay's central thesis: absurdity and meaninglessness are not objective properties of events but the reaction of an observer who cannot fit what he perceives into the web of connections available to him. Not "there is nonsense in the world, and we detect it," but "there is a boundary in the observer, and he calls nonsense everything that fails to pass through it." Perhaps we continuously produce meaning, and "meaningless" is what we call the things for which the meaning-production machinery has temporarily failed to find a template.

**The logic of transitions versus the logic of causes**—two modes of producing meaning. Awake, meaning arises through correspondence to stable models of the world—the logic of causes, the question "how is this possible?"; in dreams, through the continuity of experience: the next moment flows emotionally and associatively out of the previous one—"yes, this is exactly how it should be." Not an absence of meaning but a different type of it, working without fail: the dream world instantly incorporates any element, because awake such a takeover would be stopped by verification, and in the dream there is no one to stop it.

**The dream as a different mode of consciousness**—dreams are interesting not because they communicate truths about the world (dream-dictionary-level naivety) but because they demonstrate another mode: a large share of the control mechanisms is shut down, yet consciousness keeps generating a coherent world. If dreams expand anything, it is not the list of answers but the space of what can be perceived at all—they change the very structure of the question.

**Knowledge as control (cognitive sovereignty)**—the set of operations "name, establish connections, formalize, describe, falsify" is legitimate piece by piece, but as a whole it serves one purpose: the feeling of control over reality, with fear underneath. Cognitive sovereignty is the rule of one's own categories: we grant reality only to what we can establish it over. The list looks like a methodology and works like a sedative.

**Four masking words**—chaos, nonsense, illusion, chance. Can't classify—call it chaos; can't explain—nonsense; can't control—illusion; can't predict—chance. Four words that mask four defeats.

**The drive for meaning as a defense mechanism**—the upshot of the second section: the drive for meaning turns out to be not a search for truth but a defense. Not "I want to understand the world," but "I want the world to stay understandable enough that I never have to face its immensity."

**The teleological trap of language**—the moment talk turns to the whole, language starts humanizing it automatically: the verbs "strives," "tries," "creates," "chooses" quietly smuggle in a subject, and the Universe acquires agency, intention, and purpose that follow from nowhere. The essay shows the trap from the inside: it sprang in the middle of an argument written precisely about such traps.

**Properties of interaction instead of properties of the Universe**—the argument turned on its own categories: the same move that dismantled "nonsense" strikes "order," "chaos," "logic," "causality," "objects," "time." It may be that we know not a single property of the Universe—only the properties of our way of interacting with it; what's in question is not a portion of knowledge but its entire vocabulary.

**The dream report as the object of science**—the only access to a dream's content runs through five steps: experience → awakening → recall → translation into language → telling another person. At each step the original experience is reprocessed by a system already running on different rules, so strictly speaking we study not the dream but the story about it—like studying the ocean from the memories of a man who briefly surfaced.

**No direct access to the object**—the precise formula of the problem: not "we lack data on dreams" but "we have almost no direct access to the object of study." A shortage of data is cured by time, money, and instruments; the absence of direct access is cured by none of these, because between a dream and its researcher there always stands an awakening. The problem of dreams turns out to be a problem of the way of knowing itself: not the limits of the dream—the limits of the observer.

**A third of life, written off as noise**—sleep takes up roughly a third of all human existence[^sleepthird] and yet holds the status of an optional footnote to real life. The reason is not only the difficulty of research: sleep does not fit the basic cultural metaphor of modernity, built around action and measurable results—it produces nothing observable. Philosophy, science, and epistemology are constructed as if only wakefulness existed.

**Wakefulness as the baseline**—the assumption, adopted without discussion, that the waking state is sufficient for describing reality and that this particular mode is the norm against which all others are "altered." Logically, waking is one of consciousness's operating modes, exceptionally useful for navigation, survival, and joint activity; usefulness does not entail privileged access to the fullness of reality.

**The keyhole**—the essay's central metaphor for our epistemic situation: we stand before a locked door and examine the world through the opening meant for a key, having forgotten that the door itself exists—the way of perceiving has stopped being perceived as a way and has become reality itself. In this picture, progress does not expand knowledge but maps with ever-greater precision what has already fallen within the opening: within the opening the map is impeccable; the substitution begins where it is passed off as the map of the room. The dream is one of the few states that lets you notice the existence of the wall and the keyhole.

**The cognitive interface**—the set of formats in which consciousness is able to receive the world. Beyond it lie not unknown objects that instruments haven't reached yet, but possible forms of experience, categories, and ways of organizing reality that do not pass through the interface at all.

**The scale of confidence versus the scale of grounds**—the essay's closing formula. From the keyhole metaphor it does not follow that everything beyond the door is radically different—that is possible, not proven; what follows is something else: the scale of our confidence far exceeds the scale of our grounds for that confidence, and the depth of our own ignorance may be underestimated by several orders of magnitude.

### Borrowed External Concepts

**The predictive brain**—a model from cognitive science: the brain constantly compares the stream of perception against its model of the world and raises the alarm at a prediction error[^predictive]. In the essay—the scientific description of the daytime mechanism that registers cognitive rupture.

**REM sleep and the dampened prefrontal cortex**—the sleep stage with rapid eye movements, discovered in 1953[^rem]; in it, a region of the prefrontal cortex is selectively dampened, and with it logical control and reflective self-observation[^prefrontal]. In the essay—the physiological support for the claim that the contradiction-checking circuit is taken off duty at night.

**Children's perception of connections (Piaget)**—an observation from child psychology: a child does not infer connections between things but proceeds from their existence[^piaget]. In the essay—a daytime parallel to the dream mode.

***Participation mystique***—a concept from early-twentieth-century ethnology: in mythological thinking, connections between things are not established but presumed to already exist[^mythic]. In the essay—a second parallel: the "connection already exists" mode is known to culture not only from dreams.

**Dream yoga**—a Tibetan tradition of practice built around maintaining awareness through dreaming and deep sleep[^dreamyoga]. In the essay—evidence that people have moved toward other modes of consciousness for a long time and in earnest; not a recipe and not an invitation to practice.

**Lucid dreaming**—an experimentally recordable phenomenon of awareness inside a dream: sleepers sent pre-arranged eye-movement signals straight from REM sleep[^lucid]. In the essay—the fact of an existing research program; at the same time, in the very project of "carrying a rational subject into the dream" the essay sees a clinging to control.

**Teleology**—ascribing purpose where none has been shown; the habit of endowing the cosmos with a face, a character, and a development plan runs through the entire history of philosophy[^teleology]. In the essay—the name of the old trap the argument nearly drove into.

**Phenomenon and noumenon (Kant)**—the distinction between the world as it is given to us (phenomenon) and the "thing-in-itself" that is inaccessible to us (noumenon)[^kant]. In the essay—the historical support for the move "we know the properties of interaction, not the properties of the Universe," here pushed to the limit.

**Dream report**—a term from the methodology of dream research: what serves as "dream data" is the story told after waking[^dreamreports]. In the essay—the honest name for the fact that between the object and the data stands not an instrument but an awakening.

**The observer effect**—the situation in physics where measurement itself disturbs the system being measured[^observer]. In the essay—an analogy, not an identity: to record a dream you have to wake up, and the one who wakes is no longer the one who dreamed; the instrument of access destroys the thing it gives access to.

**Altered states of consciousness**—a collective term for dreams, deep meditation, and mystical and psychedelic experience; as early as the beginning of the 1970s, science heard a proposal to stop treating ordinary waking consciousness as the privileged reference point for studying them[^asc]. In the essay—an example of how the very grammar of a term betrays the unexamined baseline status of wakefulness.
